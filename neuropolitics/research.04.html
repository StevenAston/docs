ARTIFACT

Stream 2A: Executive dysfunction and political cognition

The neuroscience of prefrontal executive function provides a robust mechanistic substrate for many — but not all — phenomena observed in political psychology. Four independent lesion studies now converge on a single finding: prefrontal cortex damage shifts political cognition toward increased authoritarianism, reduced radicalism, and loss of ideology-policy coherence. The neurological damage documented in Streams 1A–1C (lead, COVID, TBI, alcohol, ageing, cannabis) targets precisely the circuits that govern cognitive flexibility, reality monitoring, belief updating, and emotional regulation. However, the bridge literature connecting these domains directly remains thin — perhaps 8–12 empirical studies total — and the inferential chain from population-level neurotoxic exposure to individual political cognition has never been tested end-to-end. Three of the six central project claims achieve Tier A (high integrated confidence), two reach Tier B (moderate), and one remains at Tier C. The critical gap is not in the neuroscience or the political psychology taken separately, but in the almost-empty space between them.

PART 1 — HARD NEUROSCIENCE FOUNDATION

1.1 The prefrontal cortex as executive controller of cognition

The PFC does not perform computations itself. Miller and Cohen's (2001) integrative theory — one of the most cited neuroscience papers of the 21st century — established that the PFC provides bias signals to other brain structures, guiding neural activity along pathways that establish appropriate input-output mappings for a given task. Loss of PFC function therefore does not produce a single deficit but rather the collapse of top-down control across all downstream systems: reasoning becomes stimulus-driven rather than goal-directed.

Four anatomically and functionally distinct PFC circuits govern the executive functions relevant to this review. The dorsolateral PFC (DLPFC, BA 9/46) governs working memory manipulation — not mere maintenance but the active reorganisation of held information. Barbey, Koenigs, and Grafman (2013) demonstrated in a lesion study (n=19 DLPFC patients, n=152 non-DLPFC, n=54 controls) that DLPFC damage selectively impairs manipulation of working memory contents while preserving simple maintenance, with left DLPFC necessary for verbal manipulation and right DLPFC for spatial reasoning. The ventrolateral PFC (VLPFC/right inferior frontal gyrus) governs response inhibition, established through convergent lesion and imaging evidence across thousands of participants. The orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) represents reward value in a "common currency" for decision-making and performs rapid stimulus-reinforcer reversal learning; OFC lesions cause patients to continue responding to previously rewarded stimuli after contingency reversal — even while verbalising awareness of the change. The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) detects response-level conflict and signals the DLPFC to engage cognitive control, creating a feedback loop that Botvinick and colleagues (2001) formalised computationally.

The somatic marker hypothesis provides the most direct evidence for how PFC damage degrades decision quality. Bechara and colleagues showed that VMPFC patients fail to develop anticipatory skin conductance responses to risky options on the Iowa Gambling Task and continue choosing disadvantageously — selecting from net-loss decks on approximately 60–70% of trials versus 30–35% for controls — even after explicitly knowing which strategy is correct. The mechanism is not intellectual: patients understand the odds but cannot integrate emotional signals into decisions. The somatic marker hypothesis has been challenged (Maia and McClelland, 2004, argued patients possess more explicit knowledge than originally claimed), but the core performance deficit in VMPFC patients is well-replicated across multiple laboratories.

1.2 Reality monitoring, belief formation, and aberrant salience

The cognitive neuroscience of reality monitoring — distinguishing internally generated from externally derived information — is directly relevant to political misinformation susceptibility. Johnson and Raye's (1981) framework proposed that perceived memories carry more sensory-contextual detail while imagined memories carry more cognitive operations information; when these cues are ambiguous, reality monitoring fails. Simons and colleagues (2008) localised reality monitoring to medial anterior PFC (Brodmann area 10), with fMRI demonstrating greater activation when recollecting internal versus external source context. Critically, reduced medial anterior PFC signal correlated significantly with psychosis-proneness and schizotypal traits in the same healthy sample, directly supporting a continuum from normal reality monitoring to clinical failure. Garrison and colleagues (2017) confirmed that reality monitoring impairment in schizophrenia reflects specific medial anterior PFC dysfunction dissociable from working memory impairments localised to DLPFC.

Confabulation — the genuinely believed production of false narratives — provides the strongest evidence for the PFC as a plausibility filter. Turner and colleagues (2008), studying 38 frontal patients plus controls, found that all patients scoring outside the normal confabulation range had lesions affecting either the orbital region or inferior anterior cingulate. Traditional executive function measures were not the primary predictor; the critical variable was inferior medial PFC damage specifically. Adam-Darque and colleagues (2023) extended this with an 84-patient lesion mapping study showing that right OFC plus bilateral caudate head damage predicted disorientation through impaired orbitofrontal reality filtering, with temporal context confusion as the strongest predictor (R = −0.71).

Kapur's (2003) aberrant salience framework, published in the American Journal of Psychiatry (not The Lancet as sometimes cited), provides the dopaminergic mechanism. A dysregulated hyperdopaminergic state causes aberrant assignment of salience to irrelevant environmental elements; delusions represent the cognitive system's top-down effort to make sense of these aberrantly salient experiences. Fletcher and Frith (2009) formalised this within the predictive coding framework: dopamine codes the precision (reliability) of prediction errors. When precision-weighting is aberrant, sensory noise is given excessive weight relative to prior beliefs, forcing formation of new (delusional) explanatory frameworks. Antipsychotics attenuate aberrant salience via D2 receptor blockade, providing pharmacological confirmation.

The continuum from clinical psychosis to healthy-range variation is supported but with important caveats. Kwapil and colleagues (2019) found positive schizotypy associated with self-reported aberrant salience at a large effect size (f² = 0.82), but this association did not extend to behavioural measures of perceptual salience. Barnby and colleagues (2020), in a double-blind pharmacological study (n=27), demonstrated that haloperidol (a D2 antagonist) reduced attributions of harmful intent in healthy males compared to placebo, confirming dopaminergic modulation of paranoid social inference even in non-clinical populations. However, a 2024 double-blind crossover study (n=48) found L-DOPA did not reliably increase false alarm rates for illusory pattern perception in healthy volunteers — suggesting that acute dopaminergic enhancement alone may be insufficient and that sustained disruption or interaction with pre-existing vulnerabilities is necessary.

1.3 Cognitive flexibility, perseveration, and belief updating failure

Set-shifting — the ability to abandon a previously rewarded strategy when contingencies change — is the executive function most directly relevant to ideological rigidity. Barceló and Knight (2002) demonstrated that patients with lateral PFC lesions (centred on posterior BA 9/46) showed both elevated perseverative errors (stuck-in-set) and random errors on the WCST. Glässer and colleagues (2019), using voxel-lesion symptom mapping, localised perseverative errors specifically to right PFC white matter, importantly co-localised with punishment insensitivity — suggesting that perseverative thinking reflects inability to register negative feedback from failed strategies. In schizophrenia as a model of PFC dysfunction, meta-analyses report medium-to-large effect sizes (d ≈ 0.5–1.0) for perseverative errors on the WCST.

The Bayesian brain framework provides the computational account of belief updating. Clark's (2013) predictive processing model holds that the brain constantly generates predictions and updates models via prediction error minimisation in a hierarchical structure. Gershman and Uchida (2019) demonstrated that dopamine encodes and updates beliefs within PFC-basal ganglia circuits, with the PFC representing state beliefs and policy while striatal dopamine signals integrate prediction errors. Cole and colleagues (2020) provided direct evidence that in clinical high-risk individuals, higher-level prediction errors show reduced PFC activation — specifically reduced PFC/OFC/anterior insula activation to volatility prediction errors — while simultaneously showing elevated responses to low-level prediction errors. This maps precisely onto the clinical phenotype of failure to update environmental beliefs despite contradictory evidence.

The norepinephrine system, via the locus coeruleus, modulates the precision of prediction errors. Arnsten's (2015) inverted-U model demonstrates that acute uncontrollable stress increases catecholamine release in PFC, activating feedforward calcium-PKC signalling that opens potassium channels and weakens synaptic efficacy in dendritic spines. High catecholamines simultaneously strengthen amygdala and striatum, rapidly flipping the brain from reflective (PFC-mediated) to reflexive (subcortical) control. Chronic stress produces architectural changes — dendritic spine loss — leading to persistent PFC dysfunction. This mechanism explains how chronic threat exposure could produce lasting impairment of belief-updating capacity.

The NMDA receptor pathway connects these findings to lead exposure. Lead is a potent non-competitive, voltage-independent antagonist of NMDA receptors with high affinity for the NR2A subunit. Lead blocks long-term potentiation by approximately 75% at 5μM and targets frontal cortex, nucleus accumbens, dorsal striatum, and hippocampus with dopaminergic and glutamatergic changes. Guilarte and colleagues (2015) demonstrated that developmental lead exposure recapitulates the selective loss of PV+ GABAergic interneurons and subcortical dopamine system hyperactivity seen in schizophrenia — directly connecting environmental lead exposure to the aberrant salience mechanism described above.

1.4 Pattern detection, causal narratives, and temporal discounting

Apophenia — seeing meaningful patterns where none exist — has a specific dopaminergic neural basis. Krummenacher and colleagues (2010) administered L-DOPA versus placebo to 40 healthy men in a double-blind design and found that L-DOPA shifted sceptics' response criterion to resemble that of believers in the paranormal, primarily affecting willingness to detect patterns (criterion) rather than sensitivity. This demonstrates dopaminergic modulation of the pattern-detection threshold. Whitson and Galinsky (2008) showed that lacking control increases illusory pattern perception — a conceptual link to how PFC dysfunction (reduced executive control) may increase susceptibility to false causal narratives.

Temporal discounting provides the clearest lesion evidence for how PFC damage alters preference for immediate versus delayed outcomes. McClure and colleagues (2004) identified a β system (ventral striatum, mOFC, posterior cingulate) preferentially activated by immediate rewards and a δ system (DLPFC, VLPFC, posterior parietal cortex) activated by all intertemporal choices. Sellitto, Ciaramelli, and di Pellegrino (2010) provided the first human lesion evidence that mOFC damage significantly steepens delay discounting across money, discount vouchers, and food. Su and colleagues (2025) extended this with the largest mPFC lesion study to date (n=33), finding that mPFC lesions increased temporal impulsivity and, critically, that dmPFC lesions specifically increased susceptibility to social influence on impulsive choices — directly relevant to susceptibility to impulsive political messaging.

Working memory capacity constrains the complexity of causal models people can construct. Barrett, Tugade, and Engle (2004) established that lower WMC reduces capacity for central-route persuasion processing (Petty and Cacioppo's Elaboration Likelihood Model), pushing individuals toward peripheral-route processing characterised by heuristic reliance, source cues, and emotional appeals. Attitudes formed via the peripheral route show reduced temporal persistence, reduced resistance to counter-persuasion, and weaker behavioural prediction. The PFC regions supporting central-route processing (DLPFC for WM maintenance, VLPFC for response selection, dmPFC for reflective monitoring) are the same regions supporting cognitive reappraisal, creating a double vulnerability: PFC damage simultaneously impairs analytical processing of persuasive messages and emotional regulation.

1.5 Emotional dysregulation amplifies threat-driven reasoning

The amygdala-PFC circuit provides the neural basis for emotional regulation relevant to political threat processing. Ghashghaei, Hilgetag, and Barbas (2007) established the anatomical architecture: orbital and medial PFC have the heaviest bidirectional projections with basolateral amygdala, while DLPFC connections are sparser and asymmetric (greater amygdala-to-DLPFC input than the reverse). Buhle and colleagues' (2014) meta-analysis of 48 neuroimaging studies of cognitive reappraisal found that reappraisal consistently activated dmPFC, DLPFC, and VLPFC while reliably decreasing bilateral amygdala activation — amygdala was the only region consistently modulated. The implication is stark: any reduction in PFC reappraisal capacity disinhibits amygdala threat responses.

Eysenck and colleagues' (2007) Attentional Control Theory formalised how anxiety impairs executive function. The core propositions are that anxiety increases the influence of the stimulus-driven attentional system at the expense of the goal-directed system, specifically impairing inhibition (preventing attention to irrelevant threatening stimuli) and shifting (flexible switching between tasks). Anxiety's effects on executive function correlate at approximately r ≈ −0.2 to −0.3 — a reliable if modest effect that compounds with structural PFC damage.

For in-group/out-group processing, Phelps and colleagues (2000) demonstrated that amygdala activation to outgroup faces correlates with implicit bias (IAT scores), with this automatic response modulated by PFC regulatory control. When PFC regulatory capacity is reduced, amygdala reactivity to perceived outgroup members is expected to increase, amplifying threat perception in intergroup contexts.

PART 2 — POLITICAL PSYCHOLOGY PHENOMENOLOGY

2.1 Executive function measures and political attitudes show real but small effects

The most methodologically rigorous studies using objective neuropsychological measures (not self-report) to predict political attitudes come from Zmigrod and colleagues. Their 2019 study (N=1,047) used the WCST, Remote Associates Test (RAT), and Alternative Uses Test (AUT) as objective cognitive flexibility measures. SEM showed cognitive inflexibility predicted endorsement of extremist violence (R² ≈ 29–31%). However, the WCST contribution was often non-significant (p=0.087); RAT and AUT drove most predictions. A registered direct replication by Schumann and colleagues (2021, N=1,378) found the RAT correlation confirmed but WCST accuracy was positively related to willingness to die for one's group — the opposite direction. Zmigrod and colleagues (2020) found an inverted-U relationship: both extreme-left and extreme-right showed reduced cognitive flexibility compared to moderates, challenging the simpler "rigidity of the right" narrative. A critical meta-analytic finding by Hewlett and colleagues (2021) found no correlation between self-report and neuropsychological measures of cognitive flexibility, creating a construct validity crisis for the entire literature that uses these terms interchangeably.

Jost and colleagues' (2003) meta-analysis of 88 samples (N=22,818) across 12 countries remains the field's central reference. Weighted mean correlations with conservatism ranged from r = −0.32 (openness) to r = 0.50 (death anxiety), though the death anxiety figure is likely inflated by publication bias and small terror management theory samples. The framework has been substantially qualified by Federico and Malka (2018), who demonstrated that the relationship between security/certainty needs and political preferences varies by issue domain (robust for social conservatism, null or reversed for economic conservatism) and is moderated by political engagement — among politically unsophisticated citizens, the personality-ideology link is often absent or reversed. Johnston, Lavine, and Federico (2017) showed that personality traits drive cultural attitudes, which shape economic attitudes only when people are politically engaged enough to understand partisan packaging. This fundamentally limits the universality of the motivated cognition model and suggests the neuroscience-politics link is contingent rather than direct.

Neuroimaging during political reasoning remains a small and methodologically limited literature. Kanai and colleagues (2011) reported that greater liberalism correlated with increased ACC grey matter and greater conservatism with increased right amygdala volume. The largest preregistered replication (Petalas et al., 2024, n=928) found the amygdala finding partially replicated at one-third the original effect size (r=0.068 vs. r=0.23) while the ACC finding failed to replicate entirely (p=0.685). Westen and colleagues (2006) demonstrated that motivated political reasoning activated vmPFC, ACC, posterior cingulate, and insula — with reward circuit activation (ventral striatum) upon reaching biased conclusions — but this study used only 30 male participants and has never been independently replicated with adequate power by modern fMRI standards.

2.2 Authoritarianism research converges on threat but lacks neural mechanism

The threat-authoritarianism nexus is among the more robust findings in political psychology. Onraet and colleagues' (2012) meta-analysis (N=22,086) found strong meta-analytic effects for external threat predicting RWA, replicated across US, Italian, Icelandic, Dutch, and New Zealand samples. Stenner's (2005) interaction model — that threat activates authoritarian reactions only in those with pre-existing authoritarian predispositions — adds important nuance. However, no neuroimaging study has directly tested the threat → authoritarianism pathway at the neural level. The assumption that amygdala-mediated fear processing feeds into authoritarian attitudes is inferred from Kanai's (2011) structural data and general threat-processing neuroscience, not directly tested.

A critical distinction emerges between principled conservatism and authoritarian populism. Federico, Fisher, and Deason (2011) demonstrated that the link between authoritarian predisposition and conservatism is moderated by political sophistication — among the politically unsophisticated, the mapping is weak or absent. Choma and Hanoch (2017) found lower cognitive ability predicted support for Trump specifically, and Goldsmith and Moen (2025) identified Trump "loyalists" (~10% of US adults) as characterised by conscientiousness (particularly self-discipline) rather than cognitive deficits, concluding that "political behaviour need not be motivated by policy preferences or ideology."

The study most directly linking cognition, ageing, and authoritarianism is Henry and colleagues' (2015) finding that emotion recognition (a PFC-dependent social cognitive function) was the strongest predictor of RWA in older adults, outperforming threat, education, and social dominance. This provides a plausible PFC-mediated pathway but remains a single study.

Fisher and colleagues (2021), studying 151 participants at mean age 95, found that political self-identification remained resilient despite cognitive decline (84% agreement over 6 months regardless of cognitive status), but among the cognitively impaired, ideology no longer functioned as a framework for orienting policy preferences — a striking demonstration of how cognitive decline decouples ideological labels from coherent policy positions. This is the single most important finding for the project's claims about political incoherence.

2.3 Disinformation susceptibility is predicted by analytic thinking, complicated by age

Pennycook and Rand's (2019) "lazy, not biased" finding — that CRT performance predicts misinformation discernment regardless of ideological alignment — is one of the best-replicated results in the misinformation literature, confirmed across multiple labs, countries, and platforms. The CRT-misinformation correlation is typically r = 0.10–0.30 (medium by individual-differences standards). Crucially, unlike the motivated numeracy effect (Kahan), analytic ability did not increase political polarisation around fake news.

The age-disinformation relationship presents a paradox. Guess, Nagler, and Tucker (2019) found that Facebook users over 65 shared approximately 7× more fake news articles than the youngest age group — the largest demographic effect found — using behavioural data linked to survey responses. Yet reanalysis by Brashier and Schacter (2020) found news discernment actually improved with age. The resolution appears to involve the illusory truth effect: older adults are more susceptible to repetition-induced truth inflation due to age-related source memory decline, not reasoning failure. Wang and colleagues (2016) localised the illusory truth effect to the perirhinal cortex (familiarity/fluency processing), not PFC or amygdala — the mechanism is memory-based, not executive-function-based. Lyons (2023) confirmed that prior exposure effects on false news evaluation increase with age, especially for those 60+.

Kahan's identity-protective cognition finding — that higher numeracy predicts more partisan reasoning on identity-relevant topics — has faced replication difficulties. Persson and colleagues (2021), in a preregistered replication, did not find good evidence for motivated numeracy. The tension between "lazy" and "biased" models may resolve as domain-dependent: analytic thinking protects against misinformation evaluation but may amplify polarisation on identity-threatening policy questions, though even the latter effect appears weaker than originally reported.

Ditto and colleagues' (2019) meta-analysis of 51 experimental studies (>18,000 participants) found motivated reasoning at r = 0.25 with strong support for symmetry — liberals and conservatives showed nearly identical levels of partisan bias (r = 0.235 vs. r = 0.255, respectively).

2.4 Motivated reasoning and political incoherence are well-documented phenomena

Taber and Lodge (2006) demonstrated four signatures of motivated political reasoning: prior attitude effects, disconfirmation bias, confirmation bias, and attitude polarisation from balanced exposure. Political sophistication worsened bias — the most knowledgeable participants polarised most after encountering mixed evidence. The hot cognition model (Lodge and Taber, 2005) proposes that political concepts are automatically tagged with affect upon exposure. These core findings have been replicated, though the attitude-polarisation-from-balanced-exposure effect specifically has been more elusive.

Converse's (1964) finding of low ideological constraint in mass publics remains remarkably durable. Updated analyses confirm that only ~12% of voters qualify as ideologues, with most showing low inter-issue correlations and substantial attitude instability over time. Osborne (in press) found only 65% consistency on the same issue across survey waves — barely above the 50% expected by chance. Ellis and Stimson (2012) documented "symbolic versus operational ideology" — many Americans self-identify as conservative while holding operationally liberal policy preferences — as a systematic form of political incoherence.

⚠️ Direction-of-explanation flags: The political psychology literature frequently makes or implies neurological claims without neurological evidence. Need for cognitive closure is described in terms implying neural substrates but is measured only by self-report with no established neural correlate. "Cognitive rigidity" measured by questionnaires is not equivalent to neuropsychologically-assessed executive dysfunction. The dual-process framework applied to politics assumes System 1/System 2 neural architecture maps onto political reasoning with minimal direct neuroimaging support. The threat → authoritarianism pathway is assumed to operate through amygdala-mediated processing but has never been tested at the neural level. These represent instances of the prohibited direction: political psychology being used to ground neurological claims.

PART 3 — ADJUDICATION AND CONVERGENCE

How each central claim survives evidentiary integration

The adjudication framework applies three tiers: Tier A requires a political psychology finding plus an established neurological substrate plus demonstrated impairment of that substrate by the Stream 1A–1C damage. Tier B requires a plausible but inferential substrate-damage link. Tier C holds findings as phenomenological observations without neurological grounding.

Claim 1: Perseverative anti-socialist belief as frontal set-shifting impairment

Tier rating: B (moderate confidence)

The neuroscience substrate is strong. Perseverative errors on the WCST localise to right PFC white matter (Glässer et al. 2019), co-localised with punishment insensitivity — patients persist with failed strategies because they cannot register negative feedback. DLPFC lesion patients show elevated perseverative errors that they cannot correct even when able to verbalise the correct rule (Milner 1963, 1995). The frontal-striatal circuit governing set-shifting (DLPFC → caudate → thalamus) is demonstrably damaged by lead (Cory-Slechta 1997), and lead-induced NMDA blockade impairs the synaptic plasticity underlying cognitive flexibility. Ageing produces well-documented decline in set-shifting ability. COVID preferentially impairs PFC executive function (Hampshire et al. 2024; Arnsten et al. 2023).

The political psychology evidence is weaker. No study has specifically tested whether holding outdated ideological labels (e.g., "anti-socialist" positions that predate the collapse of the Soviet Union) correlates with neuropsychological measures of perseveration. Zmigrod and colleagues found cognitive inflexibility predicts extremism rather than conservatism specifically, and the WCST-ideology relationship is inconsistent across replications. Converse's work on attitude instability suggests that for most citizens, ideological labels are only loosely connected to policy positions regardless of executive function.

What would upgrade to Tier A: A study directly measuring WCST perseverative errors (or equivalent) in a cohort born 1951–1980 and correlating these with ideological label retention despite changed policy environments, ideally with lead biomarker data or PFC volumetric measures.

Claim 2: Personality-cult attachment as OFC/VMPFC reward processing dysfunction

Tier rating: B (moderate confidence)

The neuroscience of OFC/VMPFC reward processing is well-established. OFC damage impairs stimulus-reinforcer reversal learning — patients continue responding to previously rewarded stimuli after contingency reversal despite verbal awareness (Rolls 2000, 2023). VMPFC lesion patients show "myopia for the future" on the IGT (Bechara et al. 1997). Westen and colleagues (2006) showed ventral striatum (reward circuit) activation upon reaching biased partisan conclusions. Zamboni and colleagues (2009) found political radicalism specifically associated with ventral striatum and posterior cingulate — reward prediction circuits.

The political psychology evidence for personality-cult attachment as a distinct phenomenon is emerging but limited. Goldsmith and Moen (2025) identified that Trump loyalists are characterised more by conscientiousness than cognitive deficit. The Sundahl (2023) framework distinguishes personality cults from ordinary popularity. However, Asp and colleagues (2012) directly demonstrated that vmPFC damage produces substantially higher authoritarianism and religious fundamentalism — the "False Tagging Theory" posits that PFC generates normative doubt and damage creates a "doubt deficit" increasing credulity. Nam and colleagues (2021) found that DLPFC damage linearly predicted conservatism.

The connection is plausible but inferential: OFC damage impairs the ability to reassess reward contingencies; personality-cult attachment may reflect failure to update leader evaluation in light of policy failures. However, the mechanisms generating personality cults are likely multifactorial (social identity, group dynamics, media ecosystems) and cannot be reduced to a single neural circuit.

What would upgrade to Tier A: Neuropsychological assessment (IGT, reversal learning) of participants with documented personality-cult attachment, ideally combined with PFC volumetric measurement and Stream 1 exposure biomarkers.

Claim 3: Susceptibility to simple causal narratives as PFC hypofunction plus dopaminergic aberrant salience

Tier rating: A (high integrated confidence)

This claim achieves the strongest convergence across all three evidence streams. The neuroscience is robust: PFC hypofunction reduces working memory capacity, pushing cognition toward peripheral-route processing of persuasive messages (Barrett et al. 2004; Petty and Cacioppo 1986). Dopaminergic overactivity in the ventral striatum produces apophenia — Krummenacher and colleagues (2010) directly demonstrated L-DOPA-induced criterion shifts toward false pattern detection. Su and colleagues (2025) showed dmPFC lesions specifically increase susceptibility to impulsive social influence — the largest and most recent lesion study. Reduced WMC limits the complexity of causal models people can maintain, defaulting cognition toward monocausal explanations.

The Stream 1 damage link is mechanistically direct. Lead produces mesolimbic dopamine hyperactivity (>400% DA overflow in nucleus accumbens in animal models) and recapitulates the PV+ interneuron loss and subcortical DA hyperactivity of the schizophrenia phenotype (Guilarte et al. 2015). This is the exact neurochemical profile associated with aberrant salience. COVID damages PFC function (Hampshire et al. 2024 confirmed −3 to −9 IQ-equivalent executive function deficits). The combination of PFC hypofunction (reducing filtering) and dopaminergic dysregulation (amplifying false pattern detection) in the same individuals, from the same exposures, is the strongest mechanistic convergence in the review.

The political psychology observation that susceptibility to simple causal narratives (scapegoating immigrants, feminism, or DEI for material deterioration) correlates with reduced analytic thinking is well-replicated (Pennycook and Rand 2019, r = .10–.30). The elaboration likelihood model predicts that reduced cognitive capacity → peripheral-route processing → susceptibility to emotionally compelling simple narratives.

What would upgrade further: Longitudinal cohort study correlating blood lead levels, PFC volumes, cognitive flexibility measures, and susceptibility to simple political scapegoating narratives in the target demographic.

Claim 4: Disinformation susceptibility as downstream of PFC damage from Streams 1A–1C

Tier rating: A (high integrated confidence)

The CRT-misinformation discernment relationship is one of the best-replicated findings in the field (r = .10–.30, confirmed across multiple labs, countries, and platforms). The age-disinformation relationship is behaviourally robust (7× sharing rate for 65+). The neuroscience establishes that analytic thinking capacity depends on DLPFC working memory manipulation (Barbey et al. 2013), VLPFC inhibition of prepotent responses (Aron et al. 2004/2014), and medial anterior PFC reality monitoring (Simons et al. 2008). These are precisely the regions damaged by lead (NMDA blockade → PFC), COVID (Hampshire 2024), and normative ageing.

The illusory truth effect — whereby repetition of false claims increases their perceived truth — is mediated by perirhinal cortex fluency processing (Wang et al. 2016), and older adults show increased susceptibility to this effect due to source memory decline (Brashier and Schacter 2020). Source credibility evaluation under cognitive load is impaired (Baum and Abdel Rahman 2021 showed negative emotional content overrides source evaluation at the ERP level). Asp and colleagues (2012) directly demonstrated that vmPFC damage increases credulity via the "doubt deficit" mechanism.

The one qualification is Čavojová and colleagues' (2024) finding that prior beliefs outpredict analytic thinking when both are measured — suggesting that analytic thinking is necessary but not sufficient, and that belief content (shaped by media, social networks, identity) also matters independently.

What would upgrade further: Controlled study measuring CRT, reality monitoring accuracy, and misinformation susceptibility in a cohort with quantified lead exposure history and PFC volumetric data.

Claim 5: Emotional volatility as amygdala disinhibition from reduced PFC-amygdala connectivity

Tier rating: A (high integrated confidence)

This claim has the most straightforward neuroscience-to-behaviour mapping. The amygdala-PFC circuit anatomy is precisely characterised (Ghashghaei et al. 2007). Buhle and colleagues' (2014) meta-analysis of 48 studies established that PFC reappraisal consistently decreases bilateral amygdala activation. Eysenck and colleagues' (2007) ACT formalises how anxiety impairs the goal-directed system while amplifying the stimulus-driven system (r ≈ −0.2 to −0.3). Arnsten (2015) established the molecular mechanism: high catecholamines weaken PFC while strengthening amygdala, with chronic stress producing architectural damage (dendritic spine loss) for persistent dysfunction.

Every insult documented in Streams 1A–1C demonstrably damages PFC-amygdala connectivity. Lead preferentially damages PFC and hippocampus while leaving amygdala relatively intact — the net result is disinhibited amygdala reactivity. COVID's PFC-predominant damage (Arnsten et al. 2023 specifically noted long COVID "especially afflicts the functions of the PFC" including top-down attentional control) would produce the same disinhibition pattern. Ageing involves progressive PFC volume decline with relatively preserved amygdala reactivity. TBI frequently affects prefrontal regions due to frontal anatomy's vulnerability to acceleration-deceleration injury.

The political psychology observation that threat perception drives authoritarian attitudes is robust (Onraet et al. 2012, N=22,086). The connection — damaged PFC → reduced amygdala regulation → heightened threat reactivity → increased authoritarian attitudes — follows the permitted direction (neuroscience explaining political psychology) and each link in the chain has independent evidentiary support.

What would upgrade further: Longitudinal fMRI study of PFC-amygdala functional connectivity change with age, correlated with political threat reactivity measures and Stream 1 exposure biomarkers.

Claim 6: Political incoherence as reduced working memory and cognitive flexibility

Tier rating: B (moderate confidence)

Fisher and colleagues (2021) provide the critical direct evidence: in their sample of 151 participants at mean age 95, cognitive decline produced a specific decoupling of ideological labels from policy preferences — individuals retained ideological self-identification but lost the cognitive architecture for generating coherent policy positions from that identification. This is precisely what reduced working memory capacity and cognitive flexibility would predict: holding a political identity is a simple schema requiring minimal WMC; deriving consistent policy positions requires maintaining multiple competing considerations simultaneously — the DLPFC manipulation function that Barbey and colleagues (2013) showed is specifically impaired by DLPFC damage.

Converse's (1964) finding of low ideological constraint in mass publics, confirmed by Kalmoe (2020) and Osborne (in press), establishes that political incoherence is the norm, not the exception, for most citizens. Predictors of higher constraint include education, political sophistication, and cognitive ability. The downgrade from Tier A is that Fisher's study is the only direct test, with a small and unrepresentative sample (overwhelmingly White, affluent, female, mean age 95), and the specific WMC-political coherence mechanism has not been tested with DLPFC volumetric data or neuropsychological measures in the relevant demographic (born 1951–1980).

What would upgrade to Tier A: A study in the target demographic directly measuring DLPFC function (WM manipulation tasks), cognitive flexibility (WCST), and political belief coherence (cross-issue consistency, ideology-policy alignment), ideally with lead biomarkers and PFC imaging.

What the bridge literature tells us about the project's overall trajectory

The bridge literature is remarkably thin but directionally consistent. Four independent lesion studies now converge: PFC damage shifts political cognition toward increased authoritarianism (Asp 2012), increased conservatism (Nam 2021), reduced radicalism (Cristofori 2015), and increased political involvement in conservatives specifically (2025 network study). Fisher (2021) adds that cognitive decline decouples ideology from policy coherence. This is perhaps 8–12 total empirical studies constituting the entire global literature directly linking neurological impairment to political cognition.

Three critical gaps deserve emphasis. First, no study has tested lead exposure → political cognition directly; the chain from lead to PFC damage to political reasoning remains entirely inferential at the final link. Second, no study has examined COVID cognitive effects on political reasoning; given Hampshire's (2024) finding of −3 to −9 IQ-equivalent deficits preferentially affecting executive function, this is a testable and urgent question. Third, no longitudinal study tracks cognitive decline trajectories against political attitude changes in the target 1951–1980 birth cohort. These gaps define what Stream 2B must address through empirical political behaviour data.

The direction of the evidence is unambiguous: PFC damage, from whatever cause, shifts cognition toward reduced complexity, increased credulity, heightened threat reactivity, and impaired belief coherence. The aggregate neurological burden documented in Streams 1A–1C falls precisely on the circuits governing these functions. What remains undemonstrated — and what would transform this from a well-grounded theoretical framework into an empirically validated causal model — is the direct measurement of these effects in the specific population exposed to these specific insults, engaging in these specific political behaviours. The three-tier evidentiary structure must be maintained to prevent the project from claiming more than the evidence supports while clearly identifying where the evidence already converges at high confidence.
