ARTIFACT

Multiplicative neurological damage: shared targets and inflammatory crosstalk

The primary literature supports a model of interaction between childhood lead, TBI, chronic alcohol, cannabis, ageing, and COVID-19 that is consistently super-additive in animal models but incompletely characterised in humans — best described as "at least additive, plausibly multiplicative" with the strongest mechanistic support coming from convergent neuroinflammatory pathway activation, microglial priming cascades, and cognitive reserve depletion. Four brain regions — dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, orbitofrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, and hippocampal CA1 — are damaged by all six factors, creating anatomical convergence zones where cumulative insults concentrate. The most novel claim — that neuroinflammation mobilises bone-stored lead back into circulation — is supported by individually well-established mechanistic steps but has never been tested as a complete pathway in a single study. The overall hypothesis is defensible but requires precise qualification: the evidence base consists primarily of pairwise animal studies, and no study has examined even three of the six factors simultaneously.

1. What the literature shows about additive versus multiplicative interaction

Direct combined-effect studies

The evidence base for combined neurotoxic insult interaction is dominated by Deborah Cory-Slechta's laboratory at the University of Rochester, which has produced the only systematic programme examining how developmental lead exposure interacts with a concurrent stressor. In a 2×2 factorial design, Cory-Slechta et al. (2004) demonstrated that combined maternal lead (150 ppm) plus gestational restraint stress produced potentiated effects — neurobehavioural outcomes detected in the combination group that were entirely absent with either exposure alone. This is the strongest form of super-additive interaction: the combination creates qualitatively new damage. Subsequent studies (Virgolini 2005; Rossi-George 2011; Cory-Slechta 2012, 2013) confirmed these findings were robust but profoundly sex-dependent, brain-region-specific, and outcome-measure-dependent. Males and females showed different interaction patterns, and the same combination that produced synergy in the nucleus accumbens might produce only additivity in the prefrontal cortex.

Cory-Slechta's 2005 review paper articulated the "multi-hit hypothesis": the brain can compensate for a single insult on a neural system, but when multiple insults attack the same system via different mechanisms, compensatory capacity is overwhelmed. Her 2008 review identified three interaction types across the programme: additive, synergistic (exceeding additive), and potentiated (effects only present in combination) — all observed within the same experimental system depending on which outcome was measured.

Beyond the Cory-Slechta programme, metal mixture neurotoxicity studies provide supporting evidence. Stackelberg et al. (2015) reviewed arsenic-lead-cadmium-manganese combinations and concluded the toxicological evidence suggests "a greater than additive effect of combined exposures," particularly on astrocyte apoptosis and dopaminergic systems. Papp (2006) found synergistic effects of lead-mercury-manganese combinations on cortical electrophysiology in rats.

For alcohol + TBI, Xu et al. (2025) demonstrated that adolescent alcohol consumption synergistically worsened blast-TBI-induced fear extinction deficits in mice — the first clear animal demonstration of alcohol-TBI synergy on a specific cognitive outcome. In human epidemiology, Zhang et al. (2022) examined TBI plus cardiometabolic diseases in the Swedish Twin Registry (n = 35,312) and found both additive and multiplicative super-additive interaction: RERI = 1.19 (95% CI 0.04–2.33), multiplicative interaction OR = 1.93 (p = 0.03). This is the single strongest human epidemiological demonstration of super-additive interaction between neurological risk factors for dementia.

However, other human studies show merely additive interaction. Havenon et al. (2025) found white matter hyperintensity burden plus APOE ε4 interacted additively — not multiplicatively — on dementia risk. Gottesman et al. (2022) found hypertension and brain amyloid had independent additive effects on cognitive decline with no multiplicative interaction.

Statistical framework

The preferred measures for testing biological interaction are Relative Excess Risk due to Interaction (RERI), Attributable Proportion (AP), and the Synergy Index (SI), all operating on the additive scale. VanderWeele and colleagues have established that the additive scale is "most relevant for assessing public health implications of interaction" and most closely related to Rothman's sufficient-component cause model of biological synergism. A critical methodological gap is that almost no neurotoxicology studies use RERI/AP/SI — the Cory-Slechta laboratory uses factorial ANOVA with descriptive classification, and Zhang et al. (2022) is a rare exception applying formal interaction measures.

Bottom line: Animal models consistently show super-additive or potentiated effects when insults converge on shared substrates. Human epidemiology more commonly shows additive interaction, with occasional super-additive signals. The specific six-factor combination has never been studied — even pairwise, lead + alcohol and lead + TBI have essentially no direct combined-effect literature. The evidence supports the hypothesis as plausible and mechanistically grounded but not directly demonstrated.

The prefrontal cortex emerges as the paramount convergence zone. Lead produces ~1.2% total grey matter loss inversely associated with childhood blood lead concentration, concentrated in ventrolateral PFC and anterior cingulate (Cecil et al. 2008, Cincinnati Lead Study). Chronic alcohol causes 9–13% frontal white matter reduction with documented neuronal loss (Kril et al. 1997). CTE pathology preferentially involves dorsolateral frontal cortex with tau neurofibrillary tangles in cortical layers II and III at sulcal depths (McKee et al. 2015). Cannabis thins the lateral orbitofrontal cortex over three-year follow-up (Li et al. 2025). Normal ageing hits PFC hardest — DLPFC shrinkage begins at age 40, with ~46% dendritic spine loss on prefrontal pyramidal neurons after age 50 (Peters & Folger 2012). COVID-19 causes greater grey matter thinning in orbitofrontal cortex and parahippocampal gyrus (Douaud et al. 2022).

The hippocampus shows subfield-specific vulnerability. Lead targets CA3 and dentate gyrus (disrupting CA3→CA1 synaptic transmission and reducing dentate gyrus volume). Cannabis preferentially affects CA1 and the subicular complex (Owens et al. 2020, HCP study, N=1,080). Ageing reduces neurogenesis in dentate gyrus and causes ~1% annual hippocampal volume loss. Alcohol shrinks anterior hippocampus white matter. COVID-19 produces the largest volume changes in the parahippocampal gyrus (0.2–2% greater tissue loss). Thus lead and cannabis attack different hippocampal subfields — lead at the input (dentate/CA3), cannabis at the output (CA1/subiculum) — while ageing and COVID attack the surrounding structures, collectively degrading the entire hippocampal circuit from multiple angles.

The dopaminergic system is hit by five of six factors. Lead reduces VTA dopaminergic cell numbers, alters D1/D2 receptor expression in medial PFC and nucleus accumbens, and increases striatal D2 binding. Alcohol causes volume deficits in nucleus accumbens, caudate, and putamen. Cannabis blunts striatal dopamine release long-term. Ageing produces ~10% dopamine decline per decade with D2 receptor loss of 4–8% per decade in striatum and 6–16% per decade in frontal cortex. CTE deposits tau in substantia nigra. Only COVID-19 lacks strong direct dopaminergic evidence.

The functional significance of convergence

The convergence on PFC, hippocampus, and dopaminergic circuits is not coincidental — these regions share properties that make them vulnerable. They are among the last to myelinate developmentally (the "last in, first out" principle of ageing), they have high metabolic demands, they are rich in NMDA receptors targeted by lead, and the PFC has prolonged developmental plasticity that creates an extended window of vulnerability to early-life insults. The corpus callosum is the primary site of axonal shear in TBI, the first white matter structure to age (genu), and heavily damaged by chronic alcohol (Marchiafava-Bignami disease). When six independent insults all concentrate damage on the same circuits, the probability of exceeding compensatory capacity increases non-linearly.

3. Neuroinflammatory convergence on a single master pathway

The TLR4 → NF-κB → NLRP3 cascade as universal hub

The most striking finding of this review is that four of five neuroinflammatory insults converge on the identical signalling cascade: Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) → myeloid differentiation factor 88 (MyD88) → nuclear factor kappa-B (NF-κB) → NLRP3 inflammasome → caspase-1 → interleukin-1β. Cannabis is the exception, and notably operates in the opposite direction — CB2 receptor activation suppresses this cascade.

The NLRP3 inflammasome requires two signals for full activation: Signal 1 (NF-κB-mediated transcriptional priming of NLRP3 components) and Signal 2 (assembly trigger from ROS, extracellular ATP, or potassium efflux). This two-signal requirement is mechanistically crucial: one insult can provide Signal 1 while another provides Signal 2, producing cooperative activation that exceeds what either alone would achieve. Lead activates NLRP3 via autophagy dysregulation and NF-κB (Su et al. 2021, using NLRP3 knockout mice and siRNA — minocycline reversed neurotoxicity). Alcohol activates TLR4 as a direct agonist (Alfonso-Loeches & Guerri 2012 demonstrated TLR4-knockout mice are completely protected from ethanol-induced myelin damage). TBI releases extracellular ATP as a damage-associated molecular pattern, triggering NLRP3 → caspase-1 → MMP-9 → blood-brain barrier breakdown (Anderson et al. 2025). COVID-19's Spike protein modifies exosomal miRNA cargo (miR-148a, miR-590), which suppresses USP33 and IRF9, upregulating NF-κB and TNF-α in human microglia without requiring direct viral neuroinvasion (Mishra & Banerjea 2021).

The downstream effector cytokines — TNF-α, IL-1β, and IL-6 — are elevated by all five insults. These same cytokines are the critical link to bone lead mobilisation (Section 5), creating the potential for a pathological feedback loop.

Microglial priming transforms additive inputs into multiplicative outputs

Microglial priming is the single most important mechanism supporting the multiplicative hypothesis. Defined by V. Hugh Perry and further characterised by Colm Cunningham, priming describes a process whereby a first insult sensitises microglia such that subsequent challenges produce an exaggerated, disproportionate inflammatory response. Cunningham (2012) demonstrated this definitively using a prion disease model: microglia in already-diseased brain tissue, when challenged with systemic LPS, produced abundant IL-1β and iNOS with massive neutrophil infiltration — a response completely absent when LPS was administered to normal brain tissue. Critically, the primed microglia showed no obvious morphological change before the second hit, meaning the vulnerability is invisible until triggered.

The molecular mechanism involves epigenetic reprogramming (histone modifications, hypomethylation of inflammatory gene promoters), pre-assembly of NLRP3 inflammasome components, upregulation of surface TLR4 receptors, and lower activation thresholds. Lopez-Rodriguez et al. (2021) demonstrated multi-level priming in APP/PS1 Alzheimer's mice: microglia primed for IL-1β, astrocytes primed for chemokines, and neurons rendered vulnerable to IL-1β-induced gamma rhythm disruption. The response was selectively amplified in diseased versus healthy mice receiving identical systemic inflammatory challenge.

Bouayed and Bohn (2021) explicitly applied the two-hit hypothesis to COVID-19, proposing that prior inflammatory conditions (ageing, chronic disease, early-life stressors) constitute the first hit → primed microglia → COVID-19 as second hit → exaggerated microglial cascade → severe neurological outcomes. DiSabato et al. (2016) documented that even a single TBI produces persistent reactive microglia for 6–18+ months, and repeated TBI at short intervals produces more severe neuroinflammation, axonal degeneration, and cognitive impairment than would be predicted from simple addition.

Applied to the project's six-factor model, microglial priming provides a concrete mechanism for multiplicative damage: childhood lead exposure primes microglia (first hit) → subsequent TBI encounters pre-sensitised microglia (exaggerated second-hit response + further priming) → chronic alcohol activates HMGB1/TLR4 on multiply-primed cells (third hit, amplified) → ageing adds inflammageing load on already-dysregulated microglia → COVID-19's cytokine storm impacts a brain with maximally sensitised, partially senescent microglia that produce a catastrophic inflammatory response disproportionate to the viral insult itself.

Inflammageing as force multiplier in the aged brain

Franceschi's inflammageing framework (Cevenini & Franceschi 2010) describes the chronic, low-grade systemic and neuroinflammation that accompanies normal ageing — characterised by elevated IL-6, TNF-α, and CRP without infection. In the brain, ageing produces dystrophic microglia (Angelova & Brown 2019) with a paradoxical phenotype: reduced phagocytic capacity and dendritic branching combined with increased pro-inflammatory cytokine production and ROS generation. These cells develop a senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) that propagates inflammation to neighbouring cells, creating a feed-forward loop. NF-κB signalling is significantly upregulated in aged microglia — the same pathway activated by lead, alcohol, TBI, and COVID.

The aged microglial gene expression signature substantially overlaps with the primed microglial signature (Holtman et al., meta-analysis). This means ageing itself functions as a chronic priming stimulus, and an aged brain that has previously experienced lead exposure, TBI, and alcohol-induced neuroinflammation carries microglia that have been primed multiple times across the lifespan. Morara et al. (2015) confirmed in TBI models that aged animals develop larger lesions, more neurodegeneration, and a shifted M1/M2 microglial balance compared to young animals receiving identical injuries.

Karabag et al. (2023) identified a particularly concerning mechanism: tau protein exposure induces microglial senescence, and senescent microglia worsen tau pathology — a vicious cycle connecting protein aggregation pathology to neuroinflammatory dysregulation. For individuals carrying CTE-related tau from prior subconcussive impacts, this creates a self-reinforcing degenerative cascade amplified by each of the other factors.

4. Neural reserve depletion as the mathematical basis for disproportionate damage

The threshold and cliff-effect models

Yaakov Stern's cognitive reserve framework (2002, 2009) and Robert Katzman's brain reserve concept (1988) provide the theoretical architecture for understanding why cumulative insults produce non-linear functional decline. Brain reserve (BR) is passive — total neuronal count, brain volume, synaptic density — a fixed structural buffer consumed by damage. Cognitive reserve (CR) is active — the brain's ability to use pre-existing efficient networks (neural reserve) or recruit compensatory alternate networks (neural compensation) to maintain function despite pathology. The consensus whitepaper (Stern et al. 2020) formalised these definitions.

The threshold model predicts that higher reserve delays the onset of clinical symptoms without changing the rate of decline. Evidence strongly supports this: Lenehan et al. (2014) reviewed 10 longitudinal studies and found "the majority of studies reviewed do not report significant differences in the rate of cognitive decline between lower and more highly educated individuals." Lee et al. (2019) confirmed in Parkinson's disease that higher education predicted better baseline function (HR = 0.42 for reaching advanced disease stages) but not slower disease progression.

The critical implication is the "cliff effect": because higher-reserve individuals accumulate more pathology before showing symptoms, when they finally decompensate, they do so from a position of greater accumulated damage and decline rapidly. Stern et al. (1995, 1999) demonstrated this paradox directly — AD patients with higher education showed faster cognitive decline and earlier death once clinically diagnosed. The most important finding for the multiplicative hypothesis comes from Coors et al. (2024), who demonstrated that cognitive reserve proxy (premorbid IQ) was associated with CR network expression only in individuals who also had high brain reserve. When brain reserve is sufficiently depleted, cognitive reserve cannot be effectively implemented — the "software" cannot run on degraded "hardware."

How each factor depletes reserve

Lead depletes reserve through multiple mechanisms. Cecil et al. (2008) documented ~1.2% grey matter loss inversely associated with childhood blood lead, concentrated in prefrontal and anterior cingulate regions — directly reducing BR. Lead-induced IQ deficits (~2–5 points per 10 µg/dL) reduce the primary CR proxy. Lower IQ reduces educational attainment and occupational complexity, compounding CR depletion across the lifespan. Functional MRI studies of lead-exposed adults show compensatory right-hemisphere activation replacing damaged left-hemisphere language areas — this early recruitment of compensatory networks consumes the neural compensation capacity that would otherwise buffer against later insults.

Alcohol produces brain pathology in over 70% of chronic users (Goldstein & Shelly 1980), with the "premature ageing hypothesis" supported by evidence that brain regions affected by alcohol use disorder overlap substantially with those vulnerable to normal ageing and Alzheimer's disease (Sullivan & Pfefferbaum 2019). Ferrer et al. (2021) found that CR and BDNF serve as compensatory mechanisms in early AUD but not in later phases — direct evidence of compensation exhaustion.

TBI reduces reserve measurably: individuals with lower cognitive reserve are 4.14× more likely to develop persistent post-concussion syndrome after mild TBI (Nott et al. 2019). Recovery time increases approximately 2.25× for each subsequent concussion (Benson, NHL study), directly demonstrating diminishing reserve with cumulative insults.

The Nun Study provides the most compelling direct evidence for multiplicative interaction through reserve mechanisms. Nuns with both AD pathology and small vascular strokes required fewer brain lesions to manifest dementia than those with AD pathology alone — strokes lowered the pathological threshold for clinical expression. This is precisely the mechanism proposed for the six-factor model: each insult lowers the threshold, meaning subsequent insults of equivalent magnitude produce disproportionately greater functional consequences.

The STAC model (Park & Reuter-Lorenz 2009) provides an integrating framework: the ageing brain continuously builds compensatory scaffolding, but this capacity is finite and is degraded by "depletion factors" including adverse health conditions, substance abuse, and APOE ε4. Each of the project's six factors functions as a depletion factor, collectively exhausting scaffolding capacity and leaving the brain unable to compensate for new insults.

5. Bone lead mobilisation via inflammation: mechanistically complete, empirically untested

The evidence chain

The hypothesis that neuroinflammation from TBI, alcohol, and COVID-19 mobilises bone-stored lead into circulation rests on five individually well-established mechanistic steps:

Step 1: Lead is stored in bone and is mobilisable (★★★★★). The skeleton stores ~95% of total body lead burden in adults. Lead substitutes for calcium in hydroxyapatite with a half-life of 10–30 years in cortical bone and 1–5 years in trabecular bone. Gulson et al. (1995) used stable lead isotopes to prove that 45–70% of blood lead in adults comes from bone stores, not current environmental exposure. Gulson et al. (2002) proved the mechanism definitively: bisphosphonate therapy (which inhibits osteoclastic bone resorption) reduced blood lead by ~20%, demonstrating that blocking bone resorption directly lowers blood lead from endogenous skeletal stores.

Step 2: Inflammatory cytokines upregulate RANKL and drive bone resorption (★★★★★). This is foundational osteoimmunology. TNF-α upregulates RANKL on mesenchymal cells while simultaneously inhibiting the RANKL decoy receptor osteoprotegerin (OPG), creating a dual pro-resorptive signal. IL-1β shares this capacity and is essential for the osteoclastogenic effect of TNF (Schett 2011). IL-6 upregulates RANKL expression and directly supports osteoclast differentiation (Novack 2016). The NF-κB pathway is essential for osteoclast differentiation — the same NF-κB activated by lead, alcohol, TBI, and COVID-19 in the brain is required for osteoclastogenesis in bone.

Step 3: Bone resorption releases stored lead into blood (★★★★★). NHANES data show that bone resorption markers (N-telopeptide, NTx) strongly predict blood lead levels. Postmenopausal women in the upper NTx tertile had blood lead 34% higher than the lowest tertile. Normative Aging Study data confirm that the relationship between bone lead and blood lead is significantly stronger among subjects with high bone resorption rates.

Step 4: Each factor produces systemic inflammation sufficient to affect bone (★★★★☆). Chronic alcohol causes alcohol-induced osteoporosis via IL-6/TNF-α elevation. Kupraszewicz and Brzóska (2013; PMID 23376407) demonstrated in rats that ethanol co-exposure with lead increased lead concentration in blood and decreased lead accumulation in bone — directly showing alcohol mobilises lead from bone. TBI produces systemic inflammatory response with documented effects on bone metabolism, including both osteopenia and heterotopic ossification (Bajwa, Kesavan & Mohan 2018). COVID-19's cytokine storm produces IL-6 and TNF-α at levels associated with bone loss (Tang et al. 2022), with reduced BMD documented up to one year post-infection (Alghamdi et al. 2024). Ma et al. (2023) demonstrated in a mouse acute lung injury model that pulmonary inflammation caused trabecular bone loss through CCL12 → RANKL → enhanced osteoclastogenesis — a direct demonstration of the "lung-bone axis."

Step 5: The magnitude is sufficient for neurotoxic relevance (★★★☆☆). Normative Aging Study men (average age 67) had mean tibia lead of 21.8 µg/g and patella lead of 31.2 µg/g. The O'Flaherty kinetic model predicts that tibia lead of 50 µg/g produces an endogenous blood lead contribution of ~8 µg/dL; at 100 µg/g, ~16 µg/dL. For a Baby Boomer with typical bone lead stores, a 30–50% increase in bone resorption from systemic inflammation could elevate blood lead by 1–4 µg/dL. This is neurotoxically relevant: NAS data showed that an interquartile-range increase in patella lead (20 µg/g) predicted MMSE decline equivalent to five years of ageing, and recent evidence suggests no safe threshold for lead neurotoxicity in older adults.

The feedback loop

The most provocative implication is a positive feedback loop: lead → neuroinflammation → cytokines → bone resorption → more lead in blood → more neuroinflammation. This has never been directly tested but is biochemically coherent. Lead itself activates NF-κB and NLRP3 in the brain (Step 1 of the inflammatory cascade), so remobilised lead would amplify the very neuroinflammatory signals that mobilised it.

Critical gaps

No study has measured blood lead levels before and after TBI, acute COVID-19 infection, or other discrete neuroinflammatory events. No study has directly correlated CRP or IL-6 with blood lead levels in the same older adult cohort. No human study has examined whether heavy alcohol consumption elevates blood lead from bone stores specifically (only the rat model from PMID 23376407). The complete chain from neuroinflammatory trigger through to measurable blood lead elevation has never been tested in a single study. The feedback loop from remobilised lead → amplified neuroinflammation → further bone resorption is entirely theoretical.

7. The most defensible version of the hypothesis

Given the evidence reviewed, the most precisely stated version of the multiplicative interaction hypothesis that the literature supports is:

Childhood lead exposure, subconcussive TBI, chronic alcohol use, chronic cannabis exposure, normal ageing, and COVID-19 neurological damage converge on overlapping anatomical targets — particularly prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, and dopaminergic circuits — and activate shared neuroinflammatory pathways centred on the TLR4/NF-κB/NLRP3 cascade. Animal models of pairwise combinations consistently demonstrate super-additive (synergistic or potentiated) effects when insults converge on the same neural substrates via different mechanisms. The microglial priming literature establishes that prior neuroinflammatory insults lower the activation threshold for subsequent challenges, producing exaggerated inflammatory responses — a mechanism that would transform sequential insults into disproportionately damaging events. Cognitive reserve theory predicts, and clinical data confirm, that prior reserve depletion produces non-linear functional decline in response to additional insults, with a "cliff effect" near the compensatory threshold. The complete six-factor interaction has not been directly studied and the available human epidemiological evidence is more consistent with additive than multiplicative interaction on the relative risk scale. However, the convergence of shared targets, shared inflammatory pathways, serial microglial priming, and progressive reserve depletion creates strong mechanistic grounds for expecting at minimum super-additive interaction on the absolute risk scale, with the potential for multiplicative dynamics in individuals who have accumulated insults across multiple factors. The bone-lead mobilisation pathway — whereby systemic inflammation from TBI, alcohol, and COVID-19 drives RANKL-mediated bone resorption releasing stored lead into circulation — is mechanistically coherent and supported by individually strong evidence at each step, but has not been demonstrated as a complete pathway and its quantitative magnitude, while plausible, remains uncertain.

What must be softened or qualified

  • "Multiplicative" should be replaced with "at least additive, plausibly super-additive" in formal contexts. True multiplicative interaction on the ratio scale is the strongest mathematical claim and the least empirically supported.
  • The six-factor interaction is entirely inferential. No study examines more than two of these specific factors simultaneously. The claim is constructed from pairwise evidence, general mechanistic principles, and theoretical frameworks — not from direct observation.
  • Human epidemiological evidence favours additive interaction in most studied combinations. The language should acknowledge this rather than defaulting to "multiplicative."
  • Bone-lead mobilisation via neuroinflammation should be presented as "a mechanistically supported but empirically untested pathway" rather than an established fact. The quantitative magnitude estimate (1–4 µg/dL elevation) is rough and model-dependent.
  • Cannabis is a complicating factor — CB2-mediated anti-inflammatory effects may partially counteract, rather than amplify, neuroinflammation from the other five factors. Its inclusion in the "multiplicative damage" model requires nuance about dose, timing, and cannabinoid composition.
  • The "cliff effect" (apparent stability followed by sudden decompensation) is an implication of the threshold model, but its magnitude and timing cannot be predicted from current data for the specific population of interest.

What can be stated with confidence

The convergence of six independent neurological insults on the same anatomical targets, the activation of shared neuroinflammatory master pathways by four of five insults, the well-characterised mechanism of microglial priming producing exaggerated responses to sequential challenges, and the established non-linear relationship between cumulative reserve depletion and functional decline collectively provide strong mechanistic grounds for expecting that the Baby Boomer/early Gen-X cohort carrying childhood lead burden, lifetime TBI and alcohol exposure, and now facing ageing plus COVID-19 will experience cognitive decline that exceeds what would be predicted from any single factor or simple additive model. The precise mathematical form of the interaction — additive, multiplicative, or something in between — cannot be determined from current evidence and likely varies by brain region, outcome measure, sex, and individual exposure history.

Conclusion: strong mechanism, honest uncertainty

This review finds that the multiplicative interaction hypothesis rests on a foundation of individually robust evidence — shared anatomical targets documented by volumetric MRI across all six factors, convergent neuroinflammatory pathways characterised at the molecular level, a well-validated microglial priming mechanism that transforms sequential insults into amplified responses, and cognitive reserve theory that predicts non-linear functional decline near the compensatory threshold. The novel bone-lead mobilisation pathway is mechanistically coherent through all five steps, with Kupraszewicz and Brzóska (2013) providing direct rat-model evidence that alcohol mobilises lead from bone to blood.

The critical limitation is that the evidence is constructed from components rather than observed as a whole. Cory-Slechta's multi-hit hypothesis — that the brain can compensate for a single insult but fails when multiple insults attack the same system via different mechanisms — is the best existing framework for the project's central claim. The allostatic load model (McEwen & Stellar 1993) provides complementary theoretical support for non-linear cascading effects of cumulative biological burden, though it has never been formally applied to cumulative neurotoxicant exposure.

Three empirical studies are most urgently needed to advance this hypothesis from mechanistic inference to demonstrated phenomenon: (1) measurement of blood lead levels before and after acute inflammatory events (COVID-19, surgery, acute illness) in older adults with known childhood lead exposure; (2) epidemiological analysis of cognitive decline trajectories stratified by combined lead-exposure + TBI + alcohol-use history using existing longitudinal cohorts; and (3) formal interaction analysis using RERI and synergy indices for at least three of the six factors simultaneously. Until such studies exist, the hypothesis should be characterised as strongly plausible, mechanistically well-grounded, and consistent with the available pairwise evidence — but not yet directly demonstrated.
