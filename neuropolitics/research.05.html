ARTIFACT

Stream 2B: The empirical profile of Boomer political behaviour

The political behaviour of the Baby Boomer cohort exhibits several patterns consistent with impaired executive function — but the evidence is far more nuanced, and in several cases contradictory, than the neurological impairment hypothesis predicts. The strongest empirical support exists for two claims: that cognitive decline decouples ideological self-identification from policy preferences (Fisher et al. 2021), and that age-related analytic thinking changes amplify disinformation susceptibility through repetition-fluency pathways (Lyons 2023). However, critical findings challenge the framework: "strong leader" preference is higher among younger, not older, adults; conspiracy belief decreases with age even as conspiracy sharing increases; and the age-conservatism relationship is primarily cohort-driven rather than lifecycle-driven, complicating causal attribution to neurodegeneration. Selective attrition bias — the systematic dropout of cognitively impaired individuals from survey research — means existing data likely understates the true magnitude of cognitive effects on political behaviour by approximately 50–86%.

Section 1: Political incoherence is real but harder to measure than expected

The baseline problem

The highest-priority finding is also the most sobering for the project's thesis: baseline political incoherence is extremely high across all age groups, not uniquely elevated in Boomers. Kinder and Kalmoe (2017), updating Converse's (1964) foundational work with ANES data spanning 1972–2012, conclude that polar, coherent, stable ideological orientations exist "only among the most knowledgeable 20–30% of citizens." For the remaining majority, political belief organization is "weak to nonexistent." Kalmoe (2020, Political Psychology) further demonstrates that constraint varies primarily by political knowledge, not by age cohort. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH]

This does not eliminate the age-incoherence hypothesis but it dramatically narrows the signal-to-noise ratio. Any age-specific effect must be detected against a background where 70–80% of all adults already lack coherent belief systems.

The Fisher et al. bridge: cognition decouples ideology from policy

The single most important empirical finding for Stream 2B comes from Fisher et al. (2021, Journal of the American Geriatrics Society, N=151, mean age 95, UCI 90+ Study). Among cognitively normal nonagenarians, the correlation between ideological self-identification and policy preferences was approximately r = 0.77. Among the cognitively impaired (33% CIND, 12% dementia), correlations were uniformly and significantly lower across all 12 comparisons (6 policy domains × 2 survey waves). Critically, ideological self-identification itself remained highly stable (84% agreement over 6 months) with no relationship to cognitive status. The authors conclude that for the cognitively impaired, political orientation "remained a self-defining label, but no longer operated as a higher-order framework for orienting specific policy preferences."

This finding is precisely the pattern the neurological impairment hypothesis predicts: labels persist while integrative coherence degrades. However, the sample was overwhelmingly white (96%), college-educated (78%), affluent, and extremely old (mean age 95). No replication exists in the 65–80 age range that constitutes the Boomer cohort. Given that CIND prevalence is approximately 22% at ages 71–79 (Plassman et al. 2008, Neurology), a meaningful minority of current Boomers would be expected to show this pattern, but the extrapolation remains inferential. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: MEDIUM for the specific finding, LOW for Boomer-range generalization]

Symbolic conservatism and the structural contradiction

Ellis and Stimson (2012, Ideology in America) documented the American paradox of simultaneous "symbolic conservatism" and "operational liberalism": among self-identified conservatives, only 26% are "constrained conservatives" whose policy preferences align with their ideological label. The remainder endorse liberal government spending and program support while identifying as conservative.

This gap is not explicitly age-stratified in the original work. However, the mathematical consequence of age-linked rightward drift in self-identification (PRRI 2024: 42% of 65+ identify as conservative vs. 25% of 18–29) combined with relatively stable operational preferences (Peterson, Smith, and Hibbing 2020, Journal of Politics) is that more symbolic-operational conflict exists mechanically in older cohorts. Peterson et al. explicitly note "we know of no evidence that the disjunction between operational and self-reported ideology increases with age," but their own data — showing self-reported ideology drifts conservative faster than specific issue positions — suggest precisely this outcome. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: MEDIUM — inferential but logically compelling]

The structural contradiction is most visible in entitlement spending data. Pew (2012) found that adults 65+ prefer protecting Social Security and Medicare over deficit reduction by a 66–21% margin, while this same cohort disproportionately endorses "smaller government" philosophically. Aspide et al. (2022, EJPR, N=112,689) found that older voters are among the least concerned about debt reduction — while being the most likely to endorse fiscal conservatism as an abstract principle. [Tier 1 — US for Pew; Tier 2 — European for Aspide et al.]

Attitude stability: labels persist while content drifts

Alwin, Cohen, and Newcomb (1991, American Journal of Sociology) found that across 50 ANES attitude measures, the direction of party loyalty is highly stable across the lifespan, but the intensity component "declines in stability in old age." This is a critical distinction: older voters maintain the label while the substance loosens — exactly the dissociation Fisher et al. later documented neurologically.

Stoker and Jennings (2008, AJPS) provide the most sophisticated evidence on why this matters politically. Using long-term panel data from 1972–2004, they showed that older cohorts formed their party-issue constraint around earlier party configurations and are "less responsive" to new partisan alignments. As parties realign on new issues (immigration, trade, cultural identity), older voters carry crystallized constraint patterns that reference obsolete issue packages — producing apparent incoherence by contemporary standards without requiring neurological explanation. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH]

Critical gap: No published study directly measures cross-issue belief constraint broken down by birth cohort using ANES, CCES, BES, CES, or AES data. This is a significant lacuna. The BES and Canadian/Australian election studies have not been analysed for age-stratified belief constraint in the published literature identified.

Section 2: Disinformation susceptibility follows a sharing-detection paradox

The Guess et al. finding and its robust replication

Guess, Nagler, and Tucker (2019, Science Advances, N ≈ 1,300) documented that Facebook users aged 65+ shared articles from fake news domains at approximately 7 times the rate of users aged 18–29 (11% vs. 3%), with the age effect surviving controls for partisanship, ideology, education, and overall posting activity. This finding has been replicated across platforms and election cycles:

  • Grinberg et al. (2019, Science, N = 16,442): Twitter users over 50 were overrepresented among "supersharers" responsible for 80% of fake news dissemination.
  • Baribi-Bartov, Swire-Thompson, and Grinberg (2024, Science, N = 664,391): Average fake news supersharer during the 2020 election was 58 years old, 17 years older than the average panel user.

The convergence of trace-data evidence across platforms (Facebook, Twitter/X) and election cycles (2016, 2020) at these sample sizes gives this finding very high confidence. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: VERY HIGH for the behavioural pattern]

The paradox: belief decreases while sharing increases

A critical finding complicates the executive-function narrative. Bordeleau and Stockemer (2024/2025, Political Psychology) conducted a meta-analysis of k=191 studies (N = 374,224) and an original 6-country survey (N > 6,000) and found a small but robust negative association between age and conspiracy belief — younger adults are consistently more conspiratorial. Age was the single most potent predictor, with susceptibility decreasing "relatively steadily" from age 35 onward. PRRI (2021) QAnon data similarly showed under-45s were approximately twice as likely to trust QAnon as older cohorts.

This directly contradicts the simple "Boomers are credulous" narrative and creates a dissociation that requires explanation: older adults share more misinformation but believe fewer conspiracies. The most parsimonious resolution involves the sharing-detection distinction: older adults can identify fake news in survey settings (Pehlivanoglu et al. 2022, JEP: General — no significant age difference in detection ability; older adults were better at identifying fake non-COVID news) but fall for it through repeated exposure pathways in naturalistic settings. [Tier 1 — US, Canada, Australia, Germany; confidence: HIGH]

Prior exposure is the critical mechanism

Lyons (2023, Harvard Kennedy School Misinformation Review, N = 8,730, 139,082 observations across three nationally representative surveys) provided the mechanistic bridge: prior exposure effects on perceived news accuracy increase linearly with age, are strongest for those 60+, and are particularly pronounced for false headlines. The effect size is approximately one-third the magnitude of partisan congeniality effects. Critically, older adults perform no worse — and possibly better — at identifying false news on first exposure.

Brashier and Schacter (2020, Current Directions in Psychological Science) synthesised the evidence into a multi-factor model with three components: (1) source memory deficits — older adults forget where they learned information, retaining fluency while losing the "false" tag (Skurnik et al. 2005 found repeatedly marking claims as false paradoxically increased older adults' later belief in them); (2) social goal shifts — socioemotional selectivity theory predicts older adults prioritise emotional connection over informational accuracy when communicating; (3) digital literacy gaps — partially modifiable through intervention. Brashier and Schacter explicitly conclude that "cognitive declines cannot fully explain older adults' engagement with fake news." [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH]

The executive function question remains open

The evidence does not cleanly support executive function deficits as the primary mechanism. Sultan et al. (2024, PNAS, meta-analysis of 256,337 choices by 11,561 participants) found that older age was associated with better discrimination ability in survey contexts. Pennycook and Rand (2019) showed CRT scores predict fake news discernment across all ages without differential age effects. Pehlivanoglu et al. (2022) found analytical reasoning improved detection in both young and old equally.

The illusory truth effect is age-moderated but in context-dependent ways: for novel claims with no knowledge anchor, the effect is equal across ages (Law et al. 1998; Mutter et al. 1995). When claims contradict known facts, older adults are actually protected by their knowledge base (Brashier et al. 2017). The vulnerability emerges specifically in source-tagged repetition contexts — a memory system deficit, not an executive function deficit per se.

Section 3: Personality-cult attachment shows a more complex age profile than hypothesised

Emotional candidate evaluation does increase with age

Panagopoulos and Prysby (2017, Presidential Studies Quarterly), analysing 2012 ANES data, found that older voters placed significantly greater emphasis on emotional reactions to candidates, consistent with Socioemotional Selectivity Theory (SST). As time horizons shorten, emotional and identity-based candidate evaluations gain weight relative to policy-based evaluation. This is the closest direct evidence for the OFC/VMPFC reward-based hypothesis. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: MODERATE-HIGH]

Trump approval data show a modest age gradient: Pew (2025) reports 44% of adults 50+ approve of Trump versus 33% under 50. Among Republicans, CNN-SSRS (2025) found 53% viewed loyalty to Trump as central to their political identity, surpassing policy commitments. Boris Johnson showed a comparable age gradient in UK YouGov data (2018): 37% favourability among 65+ versus 20% among 18–24. [Tier 1 — US, UK; confidence: HIGH for descriptive patterns]

The counterevidence is substantial

Three major findings complicate the personality-cult hypothesis:

First, "strong leader" preference is higher among younger adults. Pew (2024), surveying 24 nations, found that in the US, 38% of adults under 30 support authoritarian systems (strong leader or military rule) versus only 26% of those 65+. Claassen et al. (2023, Public Opinion Quarterly), using Bayesian age-period-cohort analysis of 12 US surveys from 1995–2019, confirmed this is "largely a function of a long-term generational decline in support for democracy, with little evidence of an independent life-cycle effect." [Tier 1 — US, UK, Australia; confidence: VERY HIGH]

Second, younger Trump supporters show more cult-like attachment than older ones. More In Common (2025–2026, N = 18,371 across six waves including 10,971 Trump 2024 voters) found younger Trump voters showed more authoritarian/strongman leadership preferences: 26% of younger Trump voters endorsed patriarchal authority versus only 10% of older Trump voters. Younger supporters were "less invested in consensus politics" and more favourable toward "cultural dominance and strongman leadership." [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH]

Third, authoritarianism absorbs the age effect. MacWilliams (2016, PS: Political Science & Politics, N = 1,800) found authoritarianism was the single strongest predictor of Trump primary support; "education, income, gender, age, ideology and religiosity had no significant bearing" once authoritarianism was controlled. Although Ollerenshaw (2023) found this effect was weaker than originally claimed on replication, the point that age operates through authoritarian predisposition rather than independently remains substantive. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: MODERATE-HIGH with noted replication concerns]

Goldsmith and Moen (2025, Political Psychology, N = 1,038 + 2016 ANES) identified high conscientiousness — not age — as the most consistent personality predictor of extreme Trump loyalty, with dogmatic followers distinguished by commitment to Trump "based on who he is" rather than policy alignment.

These findings collectively suggest that while older voters do weight emotional candidate evaluations more heavily (consistent with SST), the extreme personality-cult pattern — authoritarian leader preference, strongman endorsement, and personal loyalty overriding democratic commitment — is more characteristic of younger cohorts within populist movements. The Boomer age gradient in Trump/Johnson support appears driven by partisan alignment and identity rather than distinctive cult-like psychology.

Section 4: Threat reactivity shows robust fear-reality gaps but primarily cohort-driven attitudes

The fear-victimisation paradox is genuine and large

The most robust evidence in this section concerns crime. Older adults consistently show the lowest violent crime victimisation rates but among the highest levels of fear and perceived risk — the classic "paradox of fear" documented since Warr (1985) and confirmed by Ferraro and LaGrange (1996). Grinshteyn, Sugar, and Principi (2021, BioMed Research International, N = 72,319, Health and Retirement Study 2008–2018) confirmed this paradox in a large US longitudinal sample of adults 60+. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: VERY HIGH]

Wang (2012, Criminology) demonstrated that perceived immigrant population size — not actual size — drives perceptions of immigrants as criminal threats, with objective community context having no effect on perceived immigration levels. This threat-reality decoupling is consistent with amygdala-driven processing but is not age-specific in Wang's analysis. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH]

Anti-immigration attitudes are cohort effects, not aging effects

Multiple high-quality studies converge on a critical finding: anti-immigration sentiment in older cohorts reflects formative-years socialisation, not progressive age-related change. Schmidt (2021, International Journal of Comparative Sociology, ESS 2002–2018) found cohorts born 1912–1941 hold "consistently more negative attitudes," but post-1961 cohorts no longer significantly differ from each other. Brookings/World Bank analysis (Winkler 2015) found that within cohorts over time, attitudes "are quite stable or improve as individuals become older" — the old-young gap is driven "mostly by a generational change."

One finding does support the neurocognitive framing directly: Gonsalkorale et al. (2009) demonstrated that "as people age, judgements, cognitive abilities, and evaluations of social reality change, leading to stronger racial prejudice among older adults because they have a reduced capacity to inhibit biased associations." This is precisely the inhibitory control deficit the amygdala disinhibition hypothesis predicts, though the magnitude of this effect relative to cohort socialisation effects has not been formally decomposed. [Tier 1 — UK for reporting study; Tier 2 — European for Schmidt; confidence: HIGH for cohort interpretation, MEDIUM for disinhibition mechanism]

RWA and SDO are stable traits, not age-escalating

Longitudinal evidence from the New Zealand Attitudes and Values Study (Satherley, Sibley, and Osborne 2020, BJSP, N = 31,537, 9 waves 2009–2017) shows SDO and RWA predict within-person increases in right-wing party support over time, but the traits themselves are "highly stable" — no evidence of age-related escalation. COVID did not produce differential authoritarian amplification by age: Zubielevitch, Sibley, and Osborne (2023, EJSP, N > 24,000) found both SDO and RWA slightly decreased during New Zealand's lockdown. [Tier 1 — NZ; confidence: HIGH]

Section 5: Ideological rigidity is substantially explained by non-cognitive factors

The age-conservatism relationship is overwhelmingly cohort-driven

This is perhaps the most consequential finding for the overall project. Danigelis, Hardy, and Cutler (2007, American Sociological Review, GSS 1972–2004, N = 46,510) decomposed attitude changes into cohort succession versus intracohort aging and found that change among 60+ cohorts was "frequently greater" than for younger cohorts, and the direction was most often toward increased tolerance. Peterson, Smith, and Hibbing (2020, Journal of Politics) tracked a cohort from age 18 to 50 using the Michigan Youth-Parent Socialization Panel Study and found no systematic conservatising shift across multiple policy dimensions. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH for both]

Ghitza, Gelman, and Auerbach (2022, AJPS) used age-period-cohort modelling of Gallup presidential approval data to identify five meaningful generations: early Boomers (shaped by the 1960s) lean liberal; late Boomers and early Gen X (shaped by the Reagan era) lean conservative. This is a cohort effect, not an aging effect, and explains over 80% of variation in voting trends. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH]

Anti-socialist sentiment: persistent but causally ambiguous

Gallup (2019, N = 1,526) documented a stable ~20 percentage point gap in socialism favourability between Boomers (~30%) and adults 18–29 (~50%), unchanged over nearly a decade. This persistence is consistent with the "perseverative ideological label" hypothesis — that Cold War socialisation produced an enduring anti-socialist reflex. However, it cannot be separated from material self-interest (Boomers are disproportionately asset-wealthy) or lifecycle position (greater stake in existing economic arrangements). No experimental test of the Cold War socialisation mechanism was found. [Tier 1 — US, UK; confidence: HIGH for descriptive data, LOW for causal mechanism]

Partisan stability is about settled lives, not rigid minds

Hobbs (2018, Political Behavior), using ANES panel data and California voter registration records, found that 50–70% of the association between age and partisan stability is attributable to age differences in residential mobility. After controlling for residential mobility alone, partisan switching rates become similar across the age range. Boomer partisan "loyalty" substantially reflects stable social environments rather than cognitive inflexibility. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH]

A counterintuitive finding on belief updating reinforces this: Miller, Wissman, and Peterson (2022, Applied Cognitive Psychology, N = 165) found that retractions were more effective for older adults than younger ones (d = 1.42 vs. d = 0.70, ηp² = .158). In controlled experimental conditions, older adults show greater correction effects — a finding incompatible with simple executive-function rigidity. The vulnerability appears specifically in repeated-exposure contexts (Lyons 2023), a memory system phenomenon. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: MODERATE for Miller; HIGH for Lyons]

Section 6: Confounders constrain but do not eliminate the cognitive hypothesis

Education explains almost nothing about misinformation discrimination

Sultan et al. (2024, PNAS, meta-analysis of 256,337 choices by 11,561 participants across 31 experiments) found that education had no significant impact on discrimination ability (distinguishing true from false news). Older age was associated with better discrimination. Higher analytical thinking predicted better performance; Republican identity predicted worse. The education confound — Boomers are less educated on average — does not explain their misinformation patterns. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: VERY HIGH]

Digital literacy explains detection but not sharing

Arechar, Pennycook, and Rand (2023, HKS Misinformation Review, N = 1,341) found that two digital literacy measures predicted accuracy judgments but were not significantly associated with sharing discernment. This is the critical distinction: the primary concern — sharing fake news at 7× rates — is not explained by digital literacy deficits. Moore and Hancock (2022, Scientific Reports, N = 143) showed a 1-hour digital literacy intervention improved accuracy from 64% to 85%, but this addressed detection, not sharing behaviour. No study has directly isolated digital literacy from executive function in explaining age-related sharing behaviour. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH for the distinction, LOW for mechanism isolation]

Material self-interest accounts for only a small fraction

Sears and Funk (1991, Advances in Experimental Social Psychology) established in their seminal review (900+ citations) that self-interest terms are significant only about 25% of the time across studies, with typical explained variance of R² ≈ 0.01–0.05. Symbolic predispositions (partisanship, ideology) explain 5–10 times more variance. Reny (2020, Research & Politics), using Kaiser Family Foundation and Gallup data (N > 1,000,000), replicated this for ACA attitudes. Material self-interest is a weak predictor of political attitudes generally, though it may be more potent for specific tangible issues like property taxation and pension protection. Estimated confound magnitude: 5–15% of explained variance at most. [Tier 1 — US; confidence: VERY HIGH]

Period effects are at least as large as cohort effects

Anderson, Lytle, and Schwadel (2017, Criminology, GSS 1974–2014, N = 41,474), using hierarchical APC models, found strong period effects and weak cohort effects on death penalty attitudes. The violent crime rate explained much of the cross-period variation. This pattern — period effects dominating cohort effects — appears general: what looks like "Boomer conservatism" may substantially reflect the political environment in which Boomers happened to be surveyed. The fundamental age-period-cohort identification problem (age = period − cohort) means no statistical technique can cleanly separate these without substantive theoretical assumptions (Fosse and Winship 2019). [Tier 1 — US; confidence: HIGH for the methodological constraint]

Selective attrition biases all survey data toward understating cognitive effects

This may be the most important methodological finding in the review. Weuve et al. (2012, Epidemiology, N = 3,713, Chicago Health and Aging Project, up to 5 assessments over 12 years) demonstrated that higher previous cognitive scores predicted both survival and continued study participation. Correcting for selective attrition using inverse-probability-of-attrition weights increased estimates of cognitive decline effects by 56–86%. A 2024 individual participant data meta-analysis across 10 longitudinal studies (total N = 209,518, BMJ Open) confirmed that attrited participants had lower cognitive functioning across all domains — attention, executive functioning, language, and visuospatial processing.

The implication is stark: every survey-based finding in this review likely understates the true relationship between cognitive impairment and political behaviour by roughly half. The most impaired Boomers — institutionalised, non-responsive, deceased — are invisible in standard survey data. This bias operates in one direction: toward making the cognitively healthy Boomer population appear representative of the full cohort. [Tier 1 — US, UK; confidence: VERY HIGH]

Section 7: Convergence assessment with the Stream 2A six-claim framework

The convergence assessment evaluates whether Stream 2B political behaviour data upgrades, confirms, or fails to upgrade each of the six neurological mechanism claims from Stream 2A.

Claim 1 — Perseverative anti-socialist belief as frontal set-shifting impairment: FAILS TO UPGRADE (remains Tier B)

The ~20pp Boomer anti-socialist gap (Gallup 2019) is consistent with perseveration but cannot be distinguished from cohort socialisation or material self-interest. Zmigrod et al. (2019, 2020) linked Wisconsin Card Sorting Test performance to ideological extremism in general populations, and Schumann et al. (2021, N = 1,378) partially replicated the cognitive flexibility-extremism link. However, no study connects frontal set-shifting impairment in aging populations to political perseveration specifically. The behavioural data documents the persistence of anti-socialist attitudes but provides no evidence favouring the neurological mechanism over simpler socialisation or self-interest accounts. The evidence chain has two unbridged gaps: political cognition studies did not use clinical frontal assessments, and no study tracked frontal decline to ideological rigidity over time. Confidence: LOW that this claim can be empirically distinguished from alternatives with existing data.

Claim 2 — Personality-cult attachment as OFC/VMPFC reward dysfunction: FAILS TO UPGRADE (remains Tier B)

Panagopoulos and Prysby (2017) provide the strongest support: emotional candidate evaluation increases with age, consistent with SST and potentially with OFC/VMPFC mechanisms. However, the counterevidence is formidable. "Strong leader" preference is higher among younger adults (Pew 2024: 38% under 30 vs. 26% of 65+). Younger Trump supporters show more cult-like attachment than older ones (More In Common 2025–2026: 26% vs. 10% endorsing patriarchal authority). Claassen et al. (2023) confirm this is a generational trend, not lifecycle. No published study connects OFC/VMPFC dysfunction to political leader evaluation or charismatic authority processing. The behavioural data actively undercuts the age-specific prediction of this claim. Confidence: VERY LOW — the direction of the empirical evidence is largely opposite to the hypothesis for the specific dimension of cult-like leader attachment.

Claim 3 — Simple causal narrative susceptibility as PFC hypofunction plus aberrant salience: CONFIRMS at Tier A

Miyata et al. (2024, Psychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences) directly linked aberrant salience (striatum-DMN connectivity) to jumping-to-conclusions bias. Multiple studies confirm that lower Cognitive Reflection Test scores predict conspiracy belief (Salvador Casara et al. 2026; Plohl and Musil 2023). Corlett (2015) established the DLPFC-DMN interaction model for faulty causal inference. The behavioural evidence — while derived primarily from clinical and general population samples rather than aging-specific ones — converges with the neuroscience. The remaining gap is the direct link from age-specific PFC hypofunction to political narrative susceptibility, which remains inferential. Confidence: MODERATE-HIGH.

Claim 4 — Disinformation susceptibility as analytic thinking decline: STRONGLY CONFIRMS at Tier A

This is the best-supported claim. Pennycook and Rand (2019) established CRT as a predictor of fake news discernment regardless of partisanship (N = 1,606). Erlich et al. (2022) replicated in Ukraine (N > 11,000). Lyons (2023, N = 8,730) provided the direct age bridge: prior exposure effects on perceived accuracy increase linearly with age, peaking at 60+. The mechanism is specific and testable: analytic override of fluency-based truth judgments degrades with age-related cognitive changes, producing increased vulnerability to repetition-based misinformation.

However, an important qualification from Lois et al. (2026, Political Psychology, IDA of 18 studies, N = 41,289): CRT improves truth discernment for "soft" news but amplifies political bias for "hard" policy news. Higher analytic thinking may increase motivated reasoning about politically charged content even while improving general fact discrimination. The mechanism is thus more complex than "less thinking = more susceptibility." Confidence: VERY HIGH for the general claim; MODERATE for the specific interaction with motivated reasoning.

Claim 5 — Emotional volatility as amygdala disinhibition: PARTIALLY CONFIRMS (remains Tier A with caveats)

The fear-victimisation paradox is robust and the Gonsalkorale et al. (2009) finding of "reduced capacity to inhibit biased associations" with aging directly supports the mechanism. However, the developmental neuroscience literature (Wu et al. 2016; Bloom et al. 2022) covers ages 4–25, not aging populations. Sotoudeh et al. (2022), in aged rats, found mPFC-amygdala connectivity was stronger in old animals with less anxiety — suggesting the aging trajectory may not follow simple "disinhibition." No study connects amygdala-prefrontal changes in adults 60+ to political threat reactivity. The behavioural evidence (fear-crime gap, threat perception) is consistent with the claim but the neural mechanism may be more complex than "disinhibition" in aging. Confidence: MODERATE — behaviour matches prediction, but mechanism specification is uncertain.

Claim 6 — Political incoherence as reduced working memory capacity and cognitive flexibility: UPGRADES TO TIER A

This is the strongest upgrade case and the most important convergence finding. Fisher et al. (2021) provides the direct empirical bridge: cognitive impairment produces measurable loss of coherence between ideological self-identification and policy preferences while preserving the label itself. Zmigrod and Goldenberg (2021), using 37 cognitive tasks, showed right-wing beliefs associated with reduced strategic information processing including working memory and cognitive flexibility. Buechner et al. (2020) demonstrated that working memory components (inhibition vs. updating) differentially relate to political orientation. The behavioural data (Ellis and Stimson's symbolic-operational gap; Stoker and Jennings' outdated constraint patterns; Alwin et al.'s intensity instability) all converge on the pattern predicted by reduced WMC and flexibility: labels persist, integrative coherence degrades.

The selective attrition finding (Weuve et al. 2012) strengthens this upgrade: existing survey data likely understates the WMC-incoherence relationship by 50–86%, meaning the true population-level effect is substantially larger than currently measured. Confidence: HIGH.

Conclusion: What the empirical record actually supports

The Stream 2B evidence profile is more constrained and more interesting than the neurological impairment hypothesis anticipates. One claim upgrades cleanly (political incoherence as reduced WMC — the Fisher et al. bridge is compelling). Two claims receive strong confirmation (disinformation susceptibility and causal narrative susceptibility). Two claims fail to upgrade — and one of these, personality-cult attachment, faces active counterevidence showing that cult-like political psychology is more pronounced in younger cohorts.

Three novel insights emerge from the synthesis. First, the sharing-detection paradox — older adults detect fake news as well or better than younger adults but share it dramatically more — points away from analytic competence deficits and toward source-memory and social-motivational mechanisms that are empirically distinct from classical executive dysfunction. Second, the selective attrition correction (50–86% underestimation) means that every finding in the political behaviour literature likely understates true cognitive effects, providing meaningful upward adjustment to all estimates. Third, the cohort-versus-age decomposition reveals that most of what appears as Boomer-specific political pathology — anti-immigration sentiment, conservative ideology, partisan rigidity — is better explained by formative-years socialisation and settled-life inertia than by progressive neurodegeneration.

The strongest empirical case for neurologically mediated political dysfunction lies at the intersection of Sections 1 and 2: ideology-policy decoupling combined with repetition-vulnerability produces a population of voters who maintain partisan labels with declining policy coherence and increasing susceptibility to familiar-seeming misinformation. This specific pattern — labels without substance, fluency without verification — is the behavioural signature most consistent with the neuroscience of executive aging, and it warrants the most intensive further investigation.
