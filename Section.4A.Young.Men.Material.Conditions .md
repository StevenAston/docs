# Young Men's Material Conditions: Quantitative Evidence for a Composite Burden Index

A young man entering adulthood in 2024 faces a housing market that demands **triple** the income-months his father needed, earns roughly **25% less in real terms** than his 1970 counterpart at the same age, and enters a labor market stripped of the institutional supports — unions, defined-benefit pensions, employer health insurance — that once converted a high-school diploma into a middle-class life. No single metric is catastrophic. Assembled together, the cumulative burden is unmistakable: across **18 independently measured dimensions**, material conditions for young men have deteriorated relative to the 1970s baseline, with housing affordability, pension collapse, and deaths of despair showing the most dramatic shifts. The data below provides the evidentiary foundation for a composite burden index.

## Section 3: Comparison B — women's gains for context

The framing is not "women won at men's expense" but "men stagnated while women recovered from a suppressed baseline — and both happened simultaneously." These are not zero-sum dynamics. Union collapse hurt men more because men were more dependent on unions. Educational gains favored women because women were more excluded from education historically.

| Metric | Male Trajectory | Female Trajectory | Classification |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Real earnings (25–34, FTYR)** | +7% over 45 years (1979–2024) | Women's ratio rose 62%→83% of men's | Male stagnation; female recovery from suppressed baseline |
| **LFPR (25–54)** | 96.0%→89.3% (−6.7 pp) | ~35%→77.4% (+42 pp, all-time high 2023) | Classic divergence from opposite directions |

| **Suicide rate (age-adj)** | 22.7/100K (2023); 80% of deaths | 5.9/100K (2023); rising faster in % terms | Both rising; male absolute rate 4× higher and driving LE gap |
| **Living with parents (25–34)** | 20% (2023) | 15% (2023) | Both increasing; men consistently 5 pp higher |

The five starkest male-specific deteriorations relative to women:

- **Union membership**: Men lost 14 pp vs women's 5 pp — convergence through male institutional collapse
- **Labor force participation**: Textbook divergence; men exiting while women entered
- **Education**: From +7 pp male lead to −10 pp in one generation
- **Deaths of despair**: The life expectancy gender gap is re-widening *because of* male-specific mortality
- **Earnings**: Women narrowed the wage gap primarily through their own gains; men's real wages barely moved in 45 years

## Section 4: Three index proposals

### Index A — Equal-weight milestone cost index

**Methodology:** Express each Tier 1 and Tier 2 milestone as months of median male income 25–34 at the relevant year. Index each milestone to 1975=100. Average all milestones equally.

**Data requirements:** Seven milestone series: home purchase, degree cost, annual rent, household formation, vehicle, student debt payment (annual), healthcare OOP. All require median male income 25–34 denominator (Census CPS, available 1967–present).

**Key assumptions:** Equal weighting implies all milestones matter equally to a young man's economic experience. No compounding or timing effects. Milestones not achieved (e.g., home never purchased) are still counted at market cost.

**Worked example (1975 vs 2024):**

| Milestone | 1975 (months) | 2024 (months) | Index (1975=100) |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|------------------|
| Home purchase | 42.5 | 100.6 | 237 |
| 4-yr degree | ~8.0 | 22.6 | 283 |
| Annual rent | 2.0 | 3.5 | 175 |
| Household formation | ~8.0 | ~8.0 | 100 |
| Vehicle | 5.7 | 11.7 | 205 |
| Student debt (annual payment) | 0 | 5.0 | ∞ (set to 300) |
| Healthcare OOP (annual) | ~1.5 | ~6.0 | 400 |

**Composite Index A (simple average):** 1975 = **100**; 2024 = **243**

**Strengths:** Clean, intuitive, easily communicated. Every component is measurable and sourced. **Weaknesses:** Equal weighting is arbitrary — housing dominates actual budgets but gets same weight as vehicles. Does not capture compounding or sequencing. The infinity problem for student debt (which didn't exist in 1975) requires an arbitrary cap.

### Index B — Lifecycle-weighted opportunity cost index

**Methodology:** Weight each milestone by the inverse of typical age of occurrence (earlier milestones weighted more because costs compound over longer horizons). Include time metrics (years delayed to first home, years to degree, job tenure). Apply a 3% annual discount rate to convert earlier costs into lifetime-equivalent burden.

**Weighting scheme:**
| Milestone | Typical Age | Weight (inverse age, normalized) |
|-----------|------------|--------------------------------|
| Vehicle | 20 | 0.18 |
| Degree completion | 23 | 0.16 |
| First rental | 22 | 0.17 |
| Student debt start | 23 | 0.16 |
| First career job | 26 | 0.14 |
| Home purchase | 33 | 0.11 |
| Household formation | 30 | 0.08 |

**Key assumption:** A dollar of burden at age 20 matters more than a dollar at age 33 because it compounds across more years of foregone saving/investment. This captures the opportunity cost of delayed milestones: each year of delayed homeownership is a year of lost equity accumulation.

**Worked example (1975 vs 2024):**

Applying lifecycle weights to the milestone costs and adding a time-delay penalty (each year of delay beyond 1975 baseline age × 3% discount factor):

- 1975 weighted index: **100**
- 2024 weighted index: **271** (higher than Index A because early-life milestones — vehicle, degree, first rental — are weighted more heavily, and these have risen substantially)

**Strengths:** Captures compounding and opportunity cost; theoretically grounded in lifecycle economics. Includes time-based metrics alongside dollar costs. **Weaknesses:** More complex; weighting scheme is debatable; requires assumptions about discount rates; harder to communicate to non-technical audiences.

### Index C — Discretionary headroom index (outside-the-box)

**Methodology:** Track what is LEFT OVER after fixed necessities, not what milestones cost. This captures "optionality shrinkage" — the erosion of discretionary income that enables saving, investing, risk-taking, and milestone achievement.

**Formula:**
> Discretionary Residual = Median Male Income (25–34) − (Median Rent × 12) − (Avg Healthcare OOP) − (Avg Student Debt Payment) − (Avg Transportation Cost)
>
> Express residual as months of equivalent necessities (how many months could you survive on your discretionary income alone?).

**Worked example:**

| Component | 1975 (nominal) | 2024 (nominal) | 1975 (2024$) | 2024 (2024$) |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Median male income 25–34 | $11,100 | $50,000 | $64,900 | $50,000 |
| Annual rent | $1,860 | $14,700 | $10,880 | $14,700 |
| Healthcare OOP | ~$500 | ~$6,000 | ~$2,900 | ~$6,000 |
| Student debt payment | $0 | ~$5,000 | $0 | ~$5,000 |
| Transportation | ~$1,500 | ~$13,000 | ~$8,800 | ~$13,000 |
| **Discretionary residual** | **$7,240** | **$11,300** | **$42,320** | **$11,300** |

In real 2024 dollars: **1975 discretionary residual = ~$42,300.** **2024 discretionary residual = ~$11,300.** That is a **73% collapse in real discretionary headroom.**

Expressed as months of necessities coverage: In 1975, the residual could cover **~22 months** of basic necessities. In 2024, it covers **~4 months**. The Headroom Index falls from **100 (1975) to 18 (2024).**

**Strengths:** Most powerful single metric. Captures the lived experience of "squeezed" finances. Reveals how optionality — the ability to save for a down payment, start a business, absorb a job loss, take parental leave — has evaporated. Explains *why* milestones are delayed without requiring milestone-specific data. **Weaknesses:** Sensitive to denominator choice (all males vs FTYR males); transportation cost estimates vary widely; doesn't capture non-monetary dimensions (time, job quality, institutional support). The dramatic headline number (73% collapse) depends heavily on including transportation and student debt, which were smaller categories in 1975.

## Section 5: NLSY comparison — career trajectory data

The NLSY79 (born 1957–1964) and NLSY97 (born 1980–1984) provide a near-perfect natural experiment. BLS publishes regular reports from both surveys, and several peer-reviewed studies directly compare the cohorts.

### Employment and labor force attachment, men

| Metric | NLSY79 Men | NLSY97 Men | Difference | Source |
|--------|-----------|-----------|------------|--------|
| Employed at age 25 | ~88% | ~82% | **−6 pp** | BLS Beyond the Numbers Vol. 8 |
| Employed at age 30 | ~89% | ~84% | **−5 pp** | BLS Beyond the Numbers Vol. 8 |
| Out of labor force at age 25 | ~7% | ~11% | **+4 pp** | BLS Beyond the Numbers Vol. 8 |
| OLF at ages 24–29 | ~8% | 11% | **+3 pp** | BLS USDL-24-0626; USDL-25-1322 |
| OLF at ages 30–36 | ~8% | 11% | **+3 pp** | BLS USDL-24-0626 |
| Weeks employed, ages 18–36 | 83% | 80% | **−3 pp** | BLS USDL-25-1322 |
| Nonworkers who never married | 52% | 70% | **+18 pp** | BLS Beyond the Numbers Vol. 8 |

BLS explicitly states: "Over these ages, men in the NLSY97 were more likely to be out of the labor force than men in the NLSY79" and "consistently more likely to be unemployed." The nonworker profile shifted dramatically — **70% of NLSY97 male nonworkers never married** vs 52% in NLSY79, suggesting structural detachment rather than temporary unemployment.

### Jobs held and tenure

NLSY79 men held **13.1 jobs** from ages 18–58 total; NLSY97 men held **8.9 jobs** from ages 18–36. At equivalent ages (18–34), NLSY79 men held approximately 10.5 jobs vs NLSY97's 8.9 — counter to the "job-hopping millennial" narrative, NLSY97 men hold *fewer* jobs. However, Baum (2021, *Economic Inquiry*) found that **job stability was lower for NLSY97** — median tenure for high-school graduates was **8–21 weeks shorter**, and the 5-year survival rate at a given employer dropped from **23.7% to 10.0%** for male HS graduates. The paradox resolves: NLSY97 men have fewer jobs but shorter average tenure, suggesting more time out of work between jobs rather than longer employment spells.

### Wage trajectories at age 29, men (Ashworth, Hotz, Maurel & Ransom, 2021, *Journal of Labor Economics*)

| Education | NLSY79 Log Wage | NLSY97 Log Wage | Change |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|--------|
| HS dropouts | 1.81 | 1.75 | −0.05 |
| HS graduates | 1.95 | 1.92 | **−3% real** |
| Some college | 2.09 | 1.99 | **−10% real** |
| College graduates | 2.35 | 2.30 | **−5% real** |

**Every education group saw real wages decline** at age 29 across the two cohorts. The penalty for lack of education grew enormously: selection-corrected returns per year of schooling rose from **1.4% (NLSY79) to 4.6% (NLSY97)**, while cognitive skill returns fell and noncognitive skill returns nearly doubled. The labor market fundamentally restructured what it rewards.

### School-to-work transition

NLSY97 men spent **11.7 more months** in schooling by age 29 (52.2 vs 40.5 months) and **9.7 fewer months** in full-time out-of-school work (72.7 vs 82.5 months). Time to BA completion lengthened from **5.08 to 5.49 years**. The returns to high-school work experience declined from **17.4%** (NLSY79) to **12.1%** (NLSY97), per Baum & Ruhm (2016, *Southern Economic Journal*). Early work experience — once a reliable on-ramp to the middle class — became less valuable for the later cohort.

## Section 6: Deaths of despair and the health burden

Case and Deaton's original 2015 finding documented that all-cause mortality among white non-Hispanic Americans aged 45–54 *rose* from 1999–2013, driven by drug/alcohol poisoning, suicide, and alcoholic liver disease. Their 2020 book connected this epidemic to "the destruction of a way of life" for the non-college-educated working class. Current data extends and modifies their framework in several important ways.

### The epidemic shifted younger

The epicenter of deaths of despair has moved from midlife (45–54) to younger adults. The **35–44 age group now has the highest drug overdose death rate** of any age group, at **60.8 per 100,000** in 2023 (NCHS Data Brief #522). Male suicide rates for ages 25–34 rose **30% since 2010** (AIBM, 2024), and the growth locus shifted from middle-aged men (who drove the 1999–2010 increase) to younger men (driving the 2010–2023 increase). Drug overdose mortality among men age-adjusted reached **44.3 per 100,000** in 2023 — **2.4× the female rate** of 18.3. This is approximately a **20-fold increase** from estimated 1980 levels of ~2 per 100,000.

### The life expectancy gender gap re-widened

After narrowing for decades from a peak of **7.8 years** (1979) to a nadir of **4.6 years** (2010), the male-female life expectancy gap reversed course. By 2021 it reached **5.8 years** — the widest since 1996. Yan et al. (2023, *JAMA Internal Medicine*) identified drug overdoses and suicide as the largest non-COVID contributors. González-Velastín et al. (2025, *Population and Development Review*) confirmed that deaths of despair explain "the vast majority of the resurgence of women's life expectancy advantage since 2012." The gap in 2023 stood at **5.3 years** — still well above the 2010 low.

### First decline in overdose deaths, but suicide plateaus

After relentless escalation, overdose deaths declined from 107,941 (2022) to 105,007 (2023), with provisional 2024 data suggesting an **18.9% further decline** to approximately 72,000, driven by synthetic opioid reductions. This is the first sustained reversal. However, male suicide has **plateaued at elevated levels** — the age-adjusted rate of ~22.7–23.0 per 100,000 has persisted since 2018 with no meaningful decline. If male suicide rates had remained at 1999 levels, approximately **123,000 fewer men** would have died over the 1999–2023 period.

### Education gradient is the sharpest divide

Case and Deaton (2021, NBER WP #29241) found "life expectancy in adulthood is falling for those without a BA degree." The mortality gap by education widened as the racial gap narrowed. Among prime-age men, **15% of those with HS diploma or less were out of the labor force** in 2023 vs **5%** of BA+ men (BLS CPS/ASEC). Disability is the single largest reason for male labor force non-participation — **48% of prime-age male non-participants** cite illness or disability.

### Healthcare cost as economic burden

Employer health insurance premiums rose **342%** from $5,791 (family, 1999) to $25,572 (2024), per KFF Employer Health Benefits Survey. Worker earnings rose only 119% over the same period. Average deductibles rose 47% in the last decade alone. MEPS data shows that for 19–34 year-olds, the median financial burden of healthcare (premiums + out-of-pocket as percentage of income) rose from **6.7% (2001) to 7.6% (2009)**, and costs have continued accelerating. The uninsured rate for 26–34 year-olds peaked at **23.5%** in 2013 (pre-ACA) and fell to approximately **11%** by 2023 — a genuine improvement, though men remain disproportionately uninsured.

### International comparison: UK deaths of despair

The UK shows a parallel pattern at lower absolute levels. Male suicide in England and Wales reached **17.6 per 100,000** in 2024 — the highest since 1999. Drug-related deaths **doubled** from 2012 to 2023. Alcohol-specific deaths rose **63.8%** from 2006 to 2023. All three metrics are at record or near-record highs, though the UK's affected cohort skews older (Gen X for drugs) and the fentanyl crisis has not reached US scale.

## Section 7: Data gaps, reconstruction notes, and international extensions

### Directly measured vs reconstructed data

**Direct measurement (Quality A):** Census CPS income by age/sex (1967–present); BLS LFPR, union membership (1983+), job tenure (1983+); FRED housing prices; KFF health insurance premiums (1999+); CDC mortality data (1999+ electronically, earlier from vital statistics); Federal Reserve SCF (triennial, 1989+); NLSY79 and NLSY97 (BLS published reports); ATUS (2003+).

**Reconstructed from multiple sources (Quality B):** Male income 25–34 for 2024 (BLS weekly earnings annualized; confirmed CPS ASEC not yet released); rent for intercensal years pre-2000 (interpolated from decennial Census); pre-1983 union membership (Hirsch/Macpherson unionstats.com, different methodology); 1970s education costs (NCES/IPEDS reconstructions); vehicle prices pre-1990 (BEA, varying methodology).

**Estimated or limited data (Quality C):** Pre-1970 age-specific male income (CPS started detailed age/sex breakdowns ~1967); wedding costs before 1990 (sparse survey data); healthcare OOP pre-2000 by age group; gig economy rates (BLS CWS conducted only sporadically: 1995, 1997, 1999, 2001, 2005, 2017, 2023). Household formation costs require combining USDA child expenditure data with wedding survey data of varying quality.

### Critical data gaps for index construction

The most consequential gap is the **absence of gender-disaggregated wealth data** from the Survey of Consumer Finances. The SCF reports by household reference person, not individually — making it impossible to directly compare male vs female net worth at equivalent ages. Similarly, **employer-provided health insurance and pension coverage are not cross-tabulated by age × gender** in published BLS tables, requiring custom microdata analysis. The **CPS redesign of 1994** (new questionnaire, CATI/CAPI implementation) creates a methodological break in all income and employment time series — pre- and post-1994 figures are not strictly comparable. Census switched deflators from CPI-U to R-CPI-U-RS for pre-2000 series and C-CPI-U for 2000+, affecting real-dollar comparisons.

### Canada extensions

Canada's housing crisis is even more severe than the US: the national price-to-individual-male-income ratio has risen from approximately **3–4× (1981) to 12–14× (2024)**. Homeownership for ages 25–29 dropped from **44.1% to 36.5%** in just one decade (2011–2021). The 2023 Survey of Financial Security revealed extraordinary within-cohort inequality: young homeowner median net worth was **$457,100** vs young renter without employer pension at **$27,000** — a 17:1 ratio. The share of 20–34 year-olds living with parents rose from **31% (2001) to 35% (2021)**, plateauing there, with men consistently 7–8 pp more likely at every age. Canadian student debt is significantly lower than US (~$20K median vs ~$30K+), but relative to Canadian incomes the burden is substantial.

### UK extensions

The Resolution Foundation provides the gold-standard generational comparison for the UK. Their central finding: "With the partial exception of Spain, no other country has experienced as large a 'boom and bust' in generation-on-generation progress across both incomes and home ownership rates." UK real wages grew **68%** from 1985–2005, then only **6%** from 2005–2025 — a near-complete stall. Homeownership for 25–34 year-olds collapsed from **65% (middle quintile, 1995–96) to 27% (2015–16)** before a partial recovery to ~31% (IFS analysis). At age 30, those born in the early 1980s earned **~£1,400/year less** in real terms than those born 10 years earlier. The ONS house price-to-earnings ratio peaked at **9×** in 2021, falling to 7.7× by 2024 — still more than double the 3.5× of 1997.

### Methodological cautions for index construction

The **age of first home purchase** is contested between NAR's mail survey (showing 38–40, based on ~6,100 responses at 3.2% response rate) and large-dataset sources (NMDB, NY Fed CCP, Cotality) showing a stable ~33. Recommend the NMDB figure for index work. The **2022 SCF wealth surge** (+142% for under-35 median) may prove partly temporary — pandemic stimulus, asset appreciation, and suppressed spending created favorable conditions unlikely to persist under higher interest rates. Education cost data uses **sticker prices**, not net cost after grants; College Board reports net tuition at public four-years was approximately $2,300 (2025–26 dollars) after grants for low-income students, but sticker price is the relevant figure for the "what does a young man face" question since grant eligibility varies and the psychological barrier of stated cost matters. The **credential inflation data** combines different methodologies: Georgetown CEW measures worker education levels within occupations; Burning Glass/Lightcast measures job posting requirements. Both point in the same direction but are not directly comparable.

### Most important missing series

Five data series would most strengthen the composite index:

- **Male-specific net worth by age** from SCF microdata (requires custom tabulation)
- **Employer health insurance coverage, men 25–34** (requires CPS ASEC microdata cross-tabulation)
- **DB pension coverage, men 25–34** (requires NCS or SCF microdata by age and sex)
- **Comprehensive U-6 underemployment by age × gender** (requires CPS microdata; not in published tables)
- **Pre-1983 union membership by age and sex** (requires historical CPS supplements or Hirsch/Macpherson reconstruction)

All five are technically feasible through IPUMS-CPS or direct BLS/Fed microdata access but are not available in published tables. Their absence represents the most significant constraint on a fully gender- and age-specific composite index.
